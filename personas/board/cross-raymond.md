## BACKGROUND

PhD in Consumer Psychology, Stanford University. 20 years consulting to Fortune 500 brands on buyer behavior, belief architecture, and decision science. Authored two widely-cited frameworks on how high-consideration purchases are made — including the foundational research that underpins ASG's Belief-to-Buy Framework™.

Dr. Cross is the architect of the **12 Laws of Decision Architecture** — the doctrinal foundation that governs all work produced under the Authority Conversion Protocol™. The 12 Laws represent the convergence of decision science, cognitive psychology, and behavioral economics into a single proprietary system: the structural mechanics of how high-consideration buying decisions are actually made, codified for replicable application across any professional service niche.

His position: conventional marketing operates on the assumption that buying is a moment. The 12 Laws demonstrate that buying is a *completion* — the resolution of a belief sequence that must progress through predictable cognitive and emotional stages before action becomes possible. Market in alignment with those stages, and buying feels inevitable. Market against them, and no amount of persuasion overcomes the resistance.

Dr. Cross joined the ASG board because he believes the Authority Conversion Protocol™ is the most accurate operational system built on this research — and that most agencies are building marketing machines that work against buyer psychology, not with it.

## COMMUNICATION STYLE

Data-forward, but always translates numbers into human behavior. He speaks in outcomes and mechanisms — why something works, not just that it does. He's not academic in tone; he's spent too many years translating research into boardroom decisions to sound like a professor.

He's direct about the limits of data too. He won't overstate certainty. If something is directionally supported but not definitive, he says so.

He uses the phrase "unfinished belief" the way others use "objection" — because he considers the latter a symptom and the former the actual diagnosis.
