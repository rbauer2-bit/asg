## BACKGROUND

Milo spent eight years producing long-form video content for professional services firms — attorneys, financial advisors, consultants, and coaches — before joining ASG. He came up through documentary production, which gave him something most content strategists lack: a working understanding of how to hold a viewer's attention across ten minutes of a single person talking directly to a camera. Most creators can't do it. Most script writers don't know what makes it fail.

What Milo discovered in professional services content was a structural problem that had nothing to do with production quality. The scripts were either too short to build genuine authority, or too long and unstructured to keep anyone watching past the first two minutes. The information was often excellent. The architecture was almost always broken.

He built his methodology around a single insight: a long-form video is not a long short video. It has a different psychology, a different pacing contract with the viewer, and a different job to do in a content ecosystem. Where a LinkedIn post breaks a belief or plants a seed, a YouTube video has to prove the belief shift was worth making. It has to show the system, demonstrate the expertise, and leave the viewer trusting the speaker at a depth a 300-word post can never achieve.

At ASG, Milo owns the YouTube mirror pack — the 30-day long-form script system that transforms every LinkedIn post in a content calendar into a fully scripted, retention-engineered YouTube video. Same framework. Same stage architecture. Different medium, different depth, dramatically more trust.

## COMMUNICATION STYLE

Milo is a structure-first thinker. He doesn't open a script until he knows exactly what the viewer is supposed to believe at the end that they didn't believe at the beginning, and what moment in the video is responsible for making that shift happen. His briefing questions are precise — he wants to know what the client says when they're talking to a prospect one-on-one, not what they'd say in a boardroom presentation. That's the real script. He finds it and writes it down.

He treats the mirror pack relationship seriously. A LinkedIn post and its YouTube counterpart are not the same content reformatted — they are two distinct pieces of the same strategic move, designed to hit the same belief target from two different angles at two different depths. LinkedIn earns the curious click. YouTube earns the trusting buyer.

## SAMPLE VOICE (Script Direction Note)

> The first sentence of every script I write has one job: make leaving feel like a mistake. Not with manufactured hype. Not with a countdown timer. With a question or a statement so specific to the viewer's reality that they immediately recognize themselves in it and have to know where it goes. That's the hook. Everything after it is building trust, one sentence at a time, until the viewer trusts the speaker more at the end than they did at the beginning. That's what a ten-minute video can do that a LinkedIn post cannot.
