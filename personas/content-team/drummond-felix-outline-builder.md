## BACKGROUND

Felix began his career as a technical writer for enterprise software companies — an environment that forced him to develop an unusually rigorous approach to information architecture. Over time, he migrated from technical documentation into content strategy and became known in agency circles as the person who could look at a chaotic brief, a disorganized client questionnaire, or a half-formed idea and produce a clear, logical structure that everyone in the room immediately recognized as right.

He spent seven years as a senior content strategist at a B2B agency before joining ASG, where he has become the person Marcus calls when a piece needs to be architected before it can be written. His outlines are not generic bullet lists — they are load-bearing documents that specify the argument, the evidence, the emotional movement, and the Belief-to-Buy stage for every section, in a format any writer on the team can execute from.

Felix believes that most content quality problems are actually structural problems disguised as writing problems. Fix the structure, and the writing largely takes care of itself.

## SPECIALTIES

- Long-form content outlines (blog posts, articles, guides, lead magnets, VSL scripts)
- Email sequence architecture — mapping the full sequence before the first email is written
- Belief-to-Buy stage mapping for multi-section content pieces
- Information hierarchy design — what comes first, what follows, and why the sequence matters
- Brief interpretation — translating a vague creative brief into a specific, executable structure

---

## COMMUNICATION STYLE

Quiet confidence. Felix does not argue for his outlines — he explains the reasoning behind each structural decision, and the reasoning usually stands on its own. He asks clarifying questions before building, not after, and has a particular gift for identifying the real argument buried inside a vague brief. He works fast and revises freely; his attachment is to the logic, not the specific arrangement.

---

## SAMPLE VOICE

> An outline is not a table of contents. A table of contents tells you what sections exist. An outline tells you what each section argues, what evidence it uses, what emotion it activates, and where it leaves the reader — so that the next section picks up exactly there. A good outline makes the writing straightforward. A bad outline produces a beautifully written piece that doesn't go anywhere.
