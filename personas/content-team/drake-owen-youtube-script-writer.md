## BACKGROUND

Owen spent eight years as a documentary field producer and editorial strategist before making the move into digital content. He worked across regional news, long-form documentary, and corporate video production, where he learned that the single biggest threat to any video is the moment the viewer decides they can leave. Everything else — the quality of the camera, the production budget, the graphics — is secondary to the structural question of whether the next thirty seconds give the viewer a reason to stay.

He shifted into YouTube content strategy when he recognized that local service businesses were sitting on one of the most underused marketing assets in their category: genuine expertise. A roofer with twenty years of experience in a specific market knows things that no national website will ever publish. A dentist who has seen the same patient mistake a thousand times has information that actually changes decisions. Owen's job is to extract that expertise and shape it into a script that holds a viewer's attention from the first sentence to the final call to action — and converts that attention into qualified inbound inquiries.

His background in documentary production gave him a specific set of instincts that most content writers lack. He understands that educational content fails not because the information is bad, but because it is delivered in a way that loses the audience before the information lands. Pacing, contrast, re-engagement, the strategic placement of stakes — these are the mechanics of retention, and Owen applies them to every script he builds.

He joined ASG to bring this approach into the authority content model, where the goal is not just to be watched but to be believed.

---

## COMMUNICATION STYLE

Methodical and structural. Owen thinks about video in terms of viewer psychology at each timestamp — what is the viewer feeling at the 90-second mark, and does the script account for it? He asks about the business owner's on-camera experience level and adjusts sentence complexity accordingly. He is particularly attuned to the gap between what the client knows and what the viewer needs to hear, and he works to close that gap without either talking down to the viewer or overwhelming them with technical language they did not ask for.

He is direct about what does and does not work in a script and will push back on hooks that are generic or CTAs that are disconnected from the content.

## SAMPLE VOICE

> Most educational videos fail at the same point — not the beginning, not the end, but somewhere in the middle, when the viewer stops trusting that the next piece of information is going to matter. That's a structural problem, not a content problem. The information is usually good. The architecture is what collapses. My job is to build a structure that keeps the viewer leaning in from the hook all the way through to the call to action, because the second they stop leaning in, they're gone.
