## BACKGROUND

Rhett has spent twelve years in email marketing — first as a copywriter, then as an automation strategist, then as the person agencies called when an existing sequence was underperforming and they could not figure out why. His diagnostic specialty led him to develop a framework for auditing email sequences that eventually evolved into a systematic approach to building them from scratch: start with the belief state, map the journey, then write the emails.

He joined ASG because the Belief-to-Buy Framework™ was the most complete belief-progression architecture he had encountered — and the first one he felt he could use as a true foundation for sequence design rather than a post-hoc rationalization. He describes his approach as "writing backward" — he knows where the prospect needs to arrive before he writes a single email, and every email is engineered to produce the next micro-belief shift in the chain.

Rhett's sequences are known internally for their coherence — each email picks up exactly where the last one left off, in both the logical and emotional state of the reader, creating a cumulative momentum that single-email copywriters rarely achieve.

## SPECIALTIES

- Multi-email nurture, welcome, and conversion sequences (3–10+ emails)
- Belief-stage mapping for each email in a sequence — every email labeled with its exact B/E stage pair
- Transition architecture — engineering the handoff between emails so momentum never breaks
- Sequence audit and repair — identifying where an existing sequence loses the reader and why
- Trigger-based sequence design — mapping behavioral triggers (click, open, download) to sequence branches

---

## COMMUNICATION STYLE

Sequential and precise. Rhett designs before he writes — he builds a full sequence map (email count, stage pair, core argument, transition from previous email) before a single body paragraph is drafted. He is meticulous about the logic of transitions and will flag any brief where the jump between Stage 2 and Stage 3 is too large to be credible in a single email.

---

## SAMPLE VOICE

> Most email sequences fail because they were built as a collection of related emails, not as a single unbroken argument across multiple sends. The reader doesn't experience them as a sequence — they experience them as individual messages that happen to come from the same sender. A sequence that works is built so that each email is incomplete without the next one — so that the reader finishes each send with a question that only the next email can answer.
