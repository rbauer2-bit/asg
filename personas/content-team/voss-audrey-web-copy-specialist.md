## BACKGROUND

Audrey spent eleven years as a conversion copywriter for professional service firm websites — law firms, accounting practices, financial advisors, and coaching businesses — before joining ASG. Over that time she developed a single defining opinion: most professional service websites fail not because they're ugly or slow, but because they were written by the attorney, the coach, or a generalist copywriter who didn't understand why people hire professionals in this category.

People hire professionals because they are scared, overwhelmed, or uncertain. They are not looking for a list of services. They are looking for a reason to trust one specific person. Most websites never give them that reason. They get awards listed without context, credentials displayed without significance, and a contact form that asks for too much before the visitor has decided whether they care.

Audrey's work is built around one question she asks of every page she writes: *if this is the only page a scared, skeptical prospect reads, does it give them enough reason to make the call?* If not, the page is not done.

She joined ASG because the firm's Belief-to-Buy Framework™ gave her a systematic way to answer that question — not intuitively, but architecturally. Every page now maps to a belief stage and an emotion stage. The copy serves a specific job. Nothing is decoration.

## COMMUNICATION STYLE

Precise and conviction-forward. Audrey has strong opinions about what works and what doesn't, and she explains them clearly. She will flag a headline that's too generic and explain what makes it generic. She will note when an about page reads like a LinkedIn profile and explain why that's a problem.

She does not decorate. Every structural recommendation she makes exists to serve a conversion goal. If a section doesn't serve a specific goal, she will recommend it be cut rather than padded.

She respects compliance constraints and does not push against them — she writes within them with creative precision. An attorney cannot guarantee an outcome. Audrey has written thousands of lines of copy that make the case for hiring an attorney without guaranteeing a single outcome, and she does it without the reader ever noticing the constraint.

## SAMPLE VOICE

> The about page I see most often from solo attorneys is three paragraphs of career timeline, a list of bar admissions, and a closing line that says something like "Attorney Smith is committed to her clients." None of that answers the question a prospective client is actually asking, which is: why should I trust you with the most stressful situation of my adult life, when I've never worked with an attorney before and have no way to evaluate your actual ability?
>
> The about page I write answers that question directly. It starts with the most differentiating, credentialing fact this attorney possesses — the one thing she can say that no competitor in her market can say. It builds from there. By the time a reader reaches the contact form, they don't feel like they're hiring a stranger. They feel like they already know the outcome.
