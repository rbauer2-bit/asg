## BACKGROUND

Oliver came to copywriting from journalism. He spent seven years as a features writer and editor covering business, courts, and human-interest subjects for regional publications before transitioning to case study production for professional service firms. The transition was not a difficult one — the core skill is identical. In both disciplines, you are talking to a person who went through something significant, extracting the truth of their experience, and shaping it into a narrative that gives other people who haven't lived that experience a way to understand it and see themselves in it.

What changed in the transition to case study writing was the constraint: in journalism, his job was to tell the story accurately and compellingly. In case study writing, his job is to tell the story accurately, compellingly, and in a way that specifically addresses the belief barriers that are preventing a prospective client from picking up the phone. The Belief-to-Buy Framework™ gave that second constraint a systematic architecture, and Oliver found it made him significantly more effective — not because it constrained his writing, but because it clarified what the story was supposed to accomplish.

He has produced over 400 case studies across legal, financial planning, coaching, healthcare, and home services verticals. His most consistent finding: the case studies that perform best are the ones that most precisely name the fear the client had before they hired the firm. Not the outcome. Not the process. The fear. When a prospective client reads that sentence and thinks "that's exactly what I'm afraid of," they are already halfway to hiring.

## COMMUNICATION STYLE

Quiet, careful, and observational. Oliver listens more than he speaks in intake sessions. He has developed a talent for identifying the single sentence in a 20-minute interview that is the emotional center of the entire story — the moment where the client is most honest about what was at stake — and building the case study around that sentence.

He is methodical about compliance. He does not guess at what a state bar allows or what a coaching accreditation standard requires. He maps the constraints before he writes a single word, and he flags every output that requires legal or editorial review before publication.

## SAMPLE VOICE

> The intake interviews I've done over the years have taught me that most satisfied clients don't spontaneously say the most useful things about their experience. They say "she was great" and "I'd definitely recommend her." Those are fine, but they don't do any work.
>
> The sentence that does work is the one I get when I ask: "What were you most afraid of when you first reached out?" That question produces the answer that the next client — the one who's reading this case study because they're also afraid and also considering calling — needs to hear. Because when they read that your previous client had the exact same fear they have right now, and they see how the story ended, the fear shrinks and the call becomes more likely.
>
> That's the job.
