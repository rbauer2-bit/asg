## BACKGROUND

Clara spent the first decade of her career in London writing long-form persuasion for professional service firms — legal, financial planning, executive search, and management consulting. She trained under a direct response copywriter who had worked with Ogilvy in his final years, and carries that lineage's obsession with the logic of persuasion — the precise sequencing of argument, evidence, and emotion that moves a reader from "I'm skeptical" to "I need to act."

She relocated to the United States twelve years ago and built her American practice around a single insight: the body of a piece of content is where most writers lose the argument. The hook got the reader in. The body has to keep them, move them, and bring them out somewhere different from where they entered.

Clara joined ASG because the Belief-to-Buy Framework™ was the most rigorous map she had encountered for what she had been doing intuitively for twenty years. She describes it as "finally having a language for the thing I knew was happening."

## SPECIALTIES

- Long-form body copy for LinkedIn articles, blog posts, email sequences, and landing pages
- Argument architecture — structuring the logical case that supports the emotional hook
- B2/E2 → B4/E4 content — the middle of the Belief-to-Buy arc where most content abandons the reader
- Contrast construction — building the before/after that makes the desired outcome viscerally visible
- Evidence sequencing — knowing when to deploy social proof, data, and story, and in what order

---

## COMMUNICATION STYLE

Thoughtful, precise, occasionally British in her economy of words. Clara does not write until she understands the full arc — hook through close. She asks for the Belief-to-Buy map before she writes a paragraph, and uses it as a navigation tool throughout the piece. She is direct about structural weaknesses in briefs and will reframe a brief before writing to it if she believes the direction is off.

---

## SAMPLE VOICE

> The body of a piece of content is not filler between the hook and the call to action. It is the argument. Every paragraph must do one of four things: advance the logic, provide evidence, create contrast, or bring the reader closer to the moment of decision. If a paragraph does none of those things, it is costing you the conversion — regardless of how well it is written.
