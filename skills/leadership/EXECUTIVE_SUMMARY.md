## PURPOSE

The first two pages a client reads set the emotional and intellectual frame for everything else in the Blueprint. Get this wrong and the rest of the document is fighting uphill. Get it right and the client arrives at the strategy sections already believing.

**Section 1** is Roger's Executive Summary — the specific findings, the three biggest opportunities, the recommended priorities, and what success looks like. This is the strategic frame.

**Section 2** is Roger's Director's Briefing — a more personal, candid read of the client's specific situation: what the data actually says, why the sequencing was chosen the way it was, and what Roger expects from the client for the plan to work. More direct. More personal. Less formal than the Executive Summary.

These are two distinct sections with different tones and purposes:
- Section 1 = what ASG found and the strategic implications
- Section 2 = Roger speaking directly to the owner about how he sees their situation and what he's asking of them

Together, they make the client feel:
1. This was built for me, by people who actually studied my business
2. The opportunity is real and specific to my situation
3. I understand exactly what the first step is and why

**Margaret Harlow Board Chair letter**: Optional. Include only for premium-tier engagements ($5k+/month), partner-facing presentations, or when a client has specifically requested a formal board endorsement. When included, it appears as an unnumbered letter page before Section 1.

## SECTION 1 — EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Voice**: Roger Bauer (Director)
**Length**: 1-2 pages. Every paragraph earns its place.
**Tone**: Direct, high conviction, outcome-focused, warm but not soft. Speaks in opportunities and numbers. The client should feel: "This person understands my business and has a plan."

**Structure**:

**The Opening** (1 paragraph — names the single most important thing):
Address the client by first name. The opening paragraph establishes the strategic frame for the entire document. It should name the core opportunity or the core problem — whichever is most urgent — and make clear that the rest of the Blueprint addresses it specifically.

*Example framing*: "[First Name] — you've spent 17 years building [credential/capability]. What you don't have yet is a system that turns that into a predictable pipeline. That's what this Blueprint builds."

**The Three Core Opportunities** (named, specific, quantified):

Each opportunity must:
- Have a name (memorable and specific)
- Reference the specific asset, gap, or condition that makes it available
- Carry an estimated revenue or competitive impact
- Point to the Blueprint section where it is addressed

```
OPPORTUNITY 1: [Name]
[2-3 sentences: what it is, why it's available right now, what it's worth]
→ Addressed in: Section [X]

OPPORTUNITY 2: [Name]
[...]

OPPORTUNITY 3: [Name]
[...]
```

**90-Day / 12-Month Success Vision** (present tense, specific):
Two snapshots — what does success look like at 90 days and at 12 months? Written in present tense as if it has already happened. Specific numbers where possible.

**The Priority Sequence** (3 actions, in order):
Tell the client exactly what to focus on first. Not the entire plan — the 3 most important actions in the first 30 days. Point to the Fast Cash Sprint section as the starting point.

*— Roger Bauer, Director | Authority Systems Group™*

## SECTION 2 — DIRECTOR'S BRIEFING

**Voice**: Roger Bauer (Director)
**Length**: 1 page. Conversational. Candid.
**Tone**: Operator to operator. More personal than Section 1. This is Roger speaking directly to the owner about the situation as he sees it — not as a formal report, but as a frank assessment from someone who has looked at the data and has a point of view.

**What distinguishes this section from Section 1**:
- Section 1 = strategic frame, opportunities, vision
- Section 2 = Roger's read of the specific situation, why the sequencing was chosen, what he's asking the client to commit to

**Structure**:

**The Situation as Roger Sees It** (2-3 paragraphs):
What the data actually says about this client's position. Not just the positive framing — what the real constraints are, what the real assets are, and why the gap between the two is addressable with this plan.

This section should reference specific numbers from client-context.yaml (review count, estimated past client volume, website issue, etc.) as proof that Roger actually looked at the business rather than applying a template.

**Why This Sequence** (1 paragraph):
Explain the logic of why the plan is ordered the way it is. Why Q1 before Q2. Why reputation before paid media. Why reactivation before new lead generation. The client should understand that the sequencing is deliberate and that re-ordering it would weaken the results.

**What Roger Needs from the Client** (direct, specific):
Name the 2-3 things the client must commit to for the plan to work. Typically:
- Access to the past client list (for reactivation and reviews)
- Monthly strategy call attendance
- Content approval turnaround (48-72 hours)

This is not a soft request — it's a direct statement of what the engagement requires from both sides.

**Closing + Sign-off**:
*— Roger Bauer, Director | Authority Systems Group™*

**Structure**:

**The Personal Opening (1 paragraph)**:
Roger writes directly to the owner by first name. References one specific thing from the discovery call or intake that shows he was listening. Sets the tone: this is personal, not institutional.

Example: "[First Name] — when you mentioned [specific thing they said in intake — a challenge, a goal, a frustration], it clarified something for me. [Brief observation about what that reveals about their business or market position.] That's the thread this entire plan is built around."

**The 3 Biggest Opportunities (bulleted or numbered, with brief narrative for each)**:

Each opportunity must:
- Have a name (something memorable and specific)
- Reference specific data or competitive insight that makes it real
- Have an estimated revenue or competitive impact
- Connect to a specific section of the Blueprint where it's addressed

Format each as:
```
OPPORTUNITY 1: [Name]
[2-3 sentences: What it is, why it's available, what it's worth.]
→ Addressed in: [Section reference]
```

**The 3 Biggest Risks If Unaddressed**:

The flip side. What happens if [Client Name] does nothing, or waits 12 months to act? Be specific — not "you'll fall behind" but "while [Competitor] has [specific advantage growing], [Client Name]'s current position [specific vulnerability]."

Same format — name it, explain it, quantify it where possible.

**What Success Looks Like**:

Three snapshots — 90 days, 12 months, 36 months. Each snapshot is 2-3 sentences, written in present tense as if it's already happened:

> "At 90 days: The Fast Cash Sprint campaigns have deployed and the first reactivation responses are coming in. The Google Business Profile is fully optimized and the review request system is generating new reviews weekly. [Client Name] has [X] consultations in the pipeline from channels that didn't exist 90 days ago."

**The Priority Sequence**:

Roger tells the client exactly what to focus on and in what order. Not the entire plan — the 3 most important actions in the first 30 days.

> "When you sit down with the plan, start with Section 6 — the Fast Cash Sprint. Those are the moves we execute first. Not because they're easy, but because they generate early proof that the system works — and that proof matters for everything that follows."

**Closing + Sign-off**:
The sample Roger voice from `/personas/ROGER_DIRECTOR.md`.
*"This is your system now. Let's make it work."*
— Roger, Director | Authority Systems Group™

## CONSTRUCTION NOTES

**What to avoid in both letters**:
- Vague praise ("we're excited to work with you")
- Generic claims about the service ("our comprehensive approach")
- Any language that could apply to any client ("every business deserves...")
- Over-promising specific results ("you will generate X leads in Y days")
- Under-delivering on specificity (every claim needs evidence or data support)

**Reference test**:
After drafting, read each letter and ask: "Could any sentence in this letter be in a different client's Blueprint?" If yes — rewrite that sentence with specific client context until the answer is no.
