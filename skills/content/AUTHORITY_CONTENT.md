## ARTICLE PRODUCTION STANDARDS

### Length by Stage Pair

Content length should match what's needed to move the stage pair — not word count targets:

| Stage Pair | Recommended Length | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| B1/E1 (Enemy Belief + Dissatisfaction) | 800-1200 words | Pattern interrupt — needs to be tight. Too long = they lose the thread. |
| B2/E2 (Pre-Frame + Contrast) | 1000-1500 words | The reframe needs enough room to land, but not so much it meanders. |
| B3/E3 (New Worldview + Desire) | 1500-2500 words | The teaching section — this needs space to build the framework. |
| B4/E4 (Internal Alignment + Urgency) | 1200-2000 words | Case study format — story carries it, not length. |
| B5/E5 (Certainty + Relief) | 800-1500 words | Trust-close content — transparent, de-risking. Don't over-explain. |

## ARTICLE ARCHITECTURE

Every long-form article follows this structure. The names of the sections are internal — the published article uses narrative headings, not these labels.

### 1. THE HOOK (Opening paragraph)

**Rule**: Never open with "I", never open with the client's credentials, never open with a definition.

**Open in the avatar's current state**. Drop the reader into the specific situation they're living in. The first paragraph should make them think "this is about me."

Pattern for B1/E1 content:
> "[Describe the specific normalized situation they're in — their E1 state.] If that sounds familiar, [one-sentence bridge to what this piece will do for them]."

Pattern for B3/E3 content:
> "[Open with the specific desire they're starting to feel — E3.] What you're about to read will [specific promise tied to the New Worldview]."

Pattern for B5/E5 content:
> "If you're reading this, you've probably already [done the research, considered the options, talked to a few providers]. The question isn't whether you need [service] — it's whether [specific final hesitation]. Let's settle that."

### 2. THE SETUP (100-200 words)

Establishes the context and stakes. Why does this topic matter right now, for this specific avatar? Reference the trigger event or the current market/situational context.

### 3. THE BELIEF TRACK SECTION

The intellectual content — the argument, the explanation, the framework, the case.

**For Pillar 1 (Enemy Belief Dismantler)**:
- State the Enemy Belief explicitly: "Most [avatar type] believe [enemy belief]. It makes complete sense — here's why this belief formed." (Validate first)
- The pre-frame: "But here's what this assumption misses." (Shift the interpretation without attacking)
- The new interpretation: "The reason [outcome they want] is harder than it should be isn't [enemy belief] — it's [the actual reason]."

**For Pillar 2 (Authority Educator)**:
- Build the New Worldview in 3-5 clear numbered or headed sections
- Each section adds a piece to the coherent explanation
- Use specific examples, not hypotheticals — draw from niche library case patterns
- End with the assembled worldview stated plainly: "What this means is..."

**For Pillar 3 (Proof Mobilizer)**:
- Lead with the client's situation at the start (their E1 state)
- Walk through the journey — what changed, how, what happened
- State the Internal Alignment moment explicitly: "That's when [client] understood why [past attempts] hadn't worked."
- State the outcome specifically (comply with niche restrictions)

**For Pillar 4 (Trust Closer)**:
- Maximum transparency on process, timeline, costs, and what the client controls
- Address the primary final objection by name and resolve it fully
- The "what actually happens" section: step-by-step, removes all unknowns

### 4. THE EMOTION TRACK SECTION

The feeling content — the story, the contrast, the stakes.

**This section is typically woven into the Belief Track section, not separated**. The most powerful content moves both tracks simultaneously in the same sentence. However, ensure this section explicitly:
- Names or produces the appropriate emotion stage for this piece
- Has a before/after contrast (even if brief)
- Does not manufacture emotions — surfaces genuine ones

### 5. THE INTERNAL ALIGNMENT BRIDGE

For pieces targeting B3/E3 and higher: include a short section that explains why past attempts failed, through the lens of the article's new worldview.

"If you've tried [past approach] and it didn't work, here's why — and why that outcome makes complete sense given what we just walked through."

This section is what transforms an interesting article into one the reader saves and shares.

### 6. THE CLOSE

**Every article must end with one of these three close types** (matched to the stage pair):

**Insight close** (B1-B3 / E1-E3 stage pairs):
- Summarize the key insight in one powerful sentence
- Plant the next question the reader should be asking
- CTA: Next piece of content (related article, video, deeper resource)
- Soft: "If you want to understand how this applies to your situation, [link to deeper resource]."

**Proof close** (B3-B4 / E3-E4 stage pairs):
- Restate the outcome that's possible with the new worldview
- Urgency: name the cost of waiting one more [time unit]
- CTA: The consultation or next-step resource

**Convergence close** (B4-B5 / E4-E5 stage pairs):
- The Convergence Statement from the Belief Filter Map — briefly
- Name the next step and de-risk it completely
- CTA: Direct consultation booking with the process described in one sentence

## ARTICLE FORMATTING STANDARDS

**Headings**: Every 3-4 paragraphs maximum. Headings should be written to the avatar's curiosity, not the article's structure.
- Good: "The assumption that costs business owners the most in divorce"
- Bad: "Understanding Asset Division in High-Asset Divorce Cases"

**Paragraph length**: Maximum 3 sentences. Most paragraphs: 1-2.

**Opening sentence of each section**: Never starts with the section's topic noun. Opens with the human implication.
- Good: "Most business owners don't realize they've already made their biggest divorce mistake before they ever call an attorney."
- Bad: "Asset protection in divorce proceedings requires careful consideration of..."

**Call-to-action placement**: Once, at the end. Not mid-article (breaks the belief-building flow).

**Disclaimer placement** (where required by niche compliance_flags): At the bottom, below the CTA, in smaller text. Never in the opening — it breaks the hook.

## COMPLIANCE INTEGRATION

Before any article is finalized, run through the niche compliance checklist:

For ALL niches:
- [ ] No fabricated case details — use "a client of ours" if real details unavailable
- [ ] No unsubstantiated superlatives ("best", "top-rated", "most experienced")
- [ ] Outcome claims are framed as examples, not guarantees ("in our experience", "one client saw", "research shows")

For LEGAL niches specifically:
- [ ] Add disclaimer: "This content is for informational purposes only and does not constitute legal advice."
- [ ] No claims about specific legal outcomes
- [ ] No "you will" framing — use "you may be able to" or "clients in similar situations have"
- [ ] State bar restrictions on "specialist" claims — check local rules

For MEDICAL niches:
- [ ] Add disclaimer about consulting a healthcare professional
- [ ] No diagnostic claims
- [ ] FDA/FTC compliance on any treatment outcome claims
