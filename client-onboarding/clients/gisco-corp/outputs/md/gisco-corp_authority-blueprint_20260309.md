## 3E: THE SPRAY FOAM LIABILITY LANDSCAPE

*Prepared by Daniel Frost, CSO — Authority Systems Group™*

This section exists to arm Tony Long with the facts behind GISCO's no-spray-foam position. Not as talking points to lead with — but as background knowledge that makes every conversation about it more credible and more confident. ### What Actually Happened: The US Litigation Wave

Beginning in 2012, at least thirteen class action lawsuits were filed in federal district courts across Florida, Connecticut, Michigan, New Jersey, New York, Pennsylvania, and Wisconsin. These were not fringe or speculative claims. They named major manufacturers and were filed by experienced class action attorneys.

**Key cases:**

- **Slemmer v. NCFI Polyurethanes** (E.D. Pennsylvania, November 2012) — Two homeowners sued manufacturer NCFI Polyurethanes and installer McGlaughlin Spray Foam Insulation. Claims for negligence, breach of warranty, and Pennsylvania Unfair Trade Practices survived motions to dismiss. The court dismissed only the medical monitoring claim.

- **Markey v. LaPolla Industries** (E.D. New York, September 2012) — Named LaPolla Industries as the manufacturer defendant. Filed within weeks of the Slemmer case as the litigation wave accelerated.

- **Demilec class actions** (multiple jurisdictions) — Demilec LLC and its "SEALECTION 500" product was the most frequently cited brand across the 2012–2013 litigation wave. Plaintiffs alleged toxic fume emission, property damage, and that Demilec's installer network was inadequately trained and supervised.

In February 2013, plaintiffs sought consolidation of seven similar class actions before the federal Judicial Panel on Multidistrict Litigation (MDL No. 2444). The panel declined centralization — ruling the cases too individualized across different products, installers, and geographic conditions. While the MDL denial led to several voluntary dismissals, it also confirmed the individualized nature of the harm: spray foam problems vary by product, installer, temperature, humidity, and building type, making each case fact-specific.

**What this means for GISCO:** The MDL denial did not mean the cases were without merit. It meant each situation was uniquely dangerous in its own way — which is a worse problem, not a better one.

---

### The $90 Billion Whistleblower Suit

In September 2016, a federal whistleblower (qui tam) complaint was unsealed in the Northern District of California naming four of the largest chemical manufacturers in the world:

- **BASF Corporation**
- **Bayer MaterialScience LLC** (now Covestro)
- **Dow Chemical Company**
- **Huntsman International LLC**

**The core allegation:** These companies allegedly accumulated scientific and medical evidence — dating back to at least 1979 — proving that the isocyanate chemicals at the core of spray foam (MDI, TDI, PMDI) could cause permanent respiratory injury through low-level inhalation. They allegedly concealed this from the EPA for more than two decades, in violation of the Toxic Substances Control Act (TSCA) and the False Claims Act.

The suit alleged that a single drop of certain isocyanates on skin could cause permanent respiratory injury in humans — and that this was known internally while spray foam was marketed as safe.

**Scale:** The suit sought to recover $90 billion on behalf of the US government. The Department of Justice declined to intervene prior to unsealing, but did not dismiss the underlying allegations.

---

### What OSHA and the EPA Have Formally Said

This is not a legal controversy without regulatory backing. Federal agencies have confirmed the core hazard:

**OSHA** classifies isocyanates as the **leading attributable chemical cause of work-related asthma** in the United States. OSHA-published research confirms that inhalation exposures during spray foam application will typically exceed occupational exposure limits. OSHA has documented fatalities from MDI-related asthmatic reactions. A 2015 rule (29 CFR 1926 Subpart AA) mandated formal safety plans for spray foam contractors — an acknowledgment that standard practice was inadequate.

**The EPA** has formally stated that there is **no recognized safe level of isocyanate exposure for sensitized individuals** — meaning once someone develops sensitization (which can happen from a single exposure), any subsequent contact may trigger a permanent respiratory reaction. The EPA has also stated, in its own published guidance, that "the potential for off-gassing of volatile chemicals from spray polyurethane foam is not fully understood."

**NIOSH** (National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health) classified isocyanates as the primary cause of work-related asthma from chemical exposure. This is a formal classification, not an advocacy position.

---

### The Health Effects: What Homeowners Are Reporting

Across the litigation and regulatory record, documented health complaints from homeowners and building occupants include:

- Persistent headaches, including headaches lasting a full week from a single attic visit
- New-onset asthma and chemical bronchitis
- Respiratory irritation, chronic cough, chest tightness
- Blurry vision and visual halos (from amine catalyst vapors)
- Skin rashes, swelling, and joint pain
- Neurological symptoms including memory impairment
- Cardiac arrhythmia (linked to blowing agent overexposure)
- Families forced to vacate their homes while remediation occurred

In Longwood, Florida, a documented case involved a dozen families who reported their homes were rendered unlivable by toxic spray foam gases. In a widely reported Canadian case (frequently cited in US coverage), a family lived in a temporary trailer for months while their entire roof and attic structure were replaced at significant expense.

---

### The Chemical Liability That Doesn't Disappear

One critical and underappreciated aspect of spray foam liability: the isocyanate hazard does not end when the foam cures.

When cured spray foam is cut, trimmed, renovated, or demolished — even years after installation — isocyanate dust is generated. This means a building with spray foam installed today carries a future liability to every person who enters the attic for an HVAC repair, a plumber running a pipe, or a contractor doing renovation work. The risk compounds over the building's lifetime.

This is part of why GISCO's position is not just about the installation — it's about the building's long-term liability profile.

**EMAIL 3 — Day 5**
Subject: What we won't do — and why that matters

I get asked about spray foam regularly. Here's the honest answer.

GISCO does not install spray foam. We made that decision because we've followed the litigation and the regulatory record on this product for years — and what we've seen is not reassuring.

Beginning in 2012, at least thirteen class action lawsuits were filed in federal courts across the US against spray foam manufacturers including Demilec, LaPolla Industries, and NCFI Polyurethanes. In 2016, a federal whistleblower suit was unsealed naming BASF, Bayer, Dow, and Huntsman — four of the largest chemical manufacturers in the world — alleging they concealed proof of respiratory injury from the EPA for over two decades. OSHA formally classifies isocyanates (the core chemistry in spray foam) as the leading attributable chemical cause of work-related asthma in the United States. The EPA has stated, in its own guidance, that the off-gassing potential from spray foam is "not fully understood" — and that there is no recognized safe exposure level for anyone who has developed chemical sensitivity.

What we recommend instead depends on your specific situation. For most Louisville attics, properly air-sealed blown-in cellulose achieves the same or better energy performance than open-cell spray foam — with none of the liability profile, and at lower cost.

The honest answer: for most residential applications in Louisville, you don't need spray foam to get excellent results. Anyone who tells you otherwise should be able to explain exactly why your specific building requires it.
