GISCO made the decision years ago not to offer spray foam because we're not willing to put a product in your home or facility that carries that level of downstream risk. We're fine with that.

If a contractor tells you spray foam is always the best solution, ask them what happens if it goes wrong.

## SECTION 1 — WHAT'S AT STAKE

---

## SECTION 2 — HOW INFRARED THERMOGRAPHY WORKS

**Headline:** Thermal Imaging Shows What a Visual Inspection Misses

Infrared cameras detect temperature differentials — differences in surface temperature that indicate where heat is moving through or out of a building system. During an infrared inspection, our technician scans the building envelope, mechanical systems, or specific areas of concern. Anomalies appear as hot or cold spots that indicate:

- **Insulation gaps and failures** — areas where insulation has settled, shifted, or is simply absent
- **Air leakage pathways** — penetrations, bypasses, and gaps where conditioned air is escaping
- **Moisture intrusion** — wet insulation or building materials show a distinct thermal signature before visible mold or water damage appears
- **Thermal bridging** — structural members or assemblies that conduct heat and bypass the insulation system
- **Mechanical system heat loss** — pipe runs, vessels, and equipment that are underperforming from an insulation standpoint

The report you receive from a GISCO infrared inspection tells you exactly where the problems are, ranked by severity. You're not guessing. You're not deferring to someone's opinion. You have documented evidence.

## SECTION 5 — COMMON QUESTIONS

**Q: How long does an infrared inspection take?**
A: It depends on the size of the property. A typical residential inspection takes 1–2 hours. Commercial and industrial inspections are scoped individually based on facility size and systems to be assessed.

**Q: What are the conditions required for a useful thermographic inspection?**
A: For building envelope inspections, there needs to be a meaningful temperature differential between inside and outside — typically 15°F or more. This makes fall, winter, and early spring ideal times for residential and commercial envelope inspections. Mechanical system inspections can be performed year-round when systems are operating.

**Q: Will the inspection tell me how much the repairs will cost?**
A: Yes. When GISCO performs the inspection, we'll identify what we find and give you an honest estimate of what it would cost to address the issues. You get the diagnosis and the path forward in one conversation.

**Q: Do I need an infrared inspection before getting an insulation estimate?**

## SECTION 1 — WHAT'S AT STAKE

**Headline:** The Room That's Always Too Cold. The Bills That Keep Going Up. The Attic Nobody's Looked at in Years.

You've probably adapted to it — turned the thermostat up a few degrees, closed off the room that never gets comfortable, accepted that your house just runs expensive. But those aren't permanent conditions. They're symptoms of an insulation system that's no longer doing its job.

## SECTION 3 — WHAT WE INSTALL

**Headline:** The Right Material for Your Specific Situation

GISCO installs multiple insulation types for residential applications — and the right choice depends on your home's construction, existing insulation, and specific problem areas. Here's what we work with:

**Blown-In Cellulose**
The workhorse of attic retrofit insulation. Cellulose is plant-fiber based, tightly packed, and settles into irregular spaces in ways that fiberglass batts cannot. It's particularly effective for adding depth to existing attic insulation and for dense-pack installation in existing walls without opening them up. Environmentally friendly, effective, and cost-efficient.

**Fiberglass Insulation**
Fiberglass comes in both batt (roll) and blown-in forms. It's appropriate for new construction framing, attic applications, and some wall and floor situations. GISCO installs fiberglass in both commercial and residential contexts — we know when it's the right choice and when something else will perform better.

**Blanket Insulation**
Blanket-style fiberglass or mineral wool batts are the standard for framed wall cavities, floor joists, and basement ceilings. When properly installed with full coverage and no compression, batts perform at their rated R-value. When installed carelessly — and they often are — they significantly underperform. We install them correctly.

## SECTION 1 — WHY REMOVAL COMES FIRST

**Headline:** Adding Insulation Over a Problem Doesn't Fix the Problem

There's a temptation — sometimes encouraged by contractors who want the fastest path to a job — to blow new insulation over old, compromised material. In some situations, that's appropriate. In others, it's the wrong call.

Insulation that has been saturated with moisture, contaminated by pest activity, or that has structurally failed (collapsed, shifted significantly, or lost its integrity) needs to come out before new material goes in. Installing over a compromised substrate produces a compromised result — and it leaves the underlying problem unaddressed.

GISCO's assessment will tell you clearly which situation you're in. If removal is warranted, we'll do it. If it isn't, we'll tell you that instead.

---

## SECTION 2 — WHEN REMOVAL & REPLACEMENT IS THE RIGHT CALL

**Headline:** Signs That Point to Removal

- **Rodent or pest activity** — Contaminated insulation is a health concern. Pest waste, nesting material, and damage from activity require full removal before new insulation is installed.
- **Moisture damage** — Insulation soaked from a roof leak, condensation problem, or plumbing issue loses its thermal performance and can harbor mold. The insulation needs to come out so the moisture source can be addressed and the space can dry before new material goes in.
- **Age and settlement** — Older fiberglass batts can lose loft and structural integrity over time. In some cases, removal and fresh installation outperforms adding material on top of significantly degraded substrate.
- **Improper original installation** — Compressed batts, gaps around penetrations, or the wrong material type in the wrong location. Sometimes starting clean is more cost-effective than trying to correct around poor original work.
- **Pre-renovation assessment** — Before finishing a basement, adding conditioned space, or undertaking significant structural work, clearing out old insulation allows for a fresh, properly specified installation.

## H1

Air Sealing — Louisville, KY
The Step Most Insulation Contractors Skip — And Why It Matters

**Subhead:**
Insulation slows heat transfer. Air sealing stops air movement. Both are necessary. Without air sealing, even properly installed insulation underperforms because conditioned air is bypassing it through gaps, penetrations, and top-plate openings that insulation material alone doesn't block.

**CTA Button:** Get a Free Assessment

---

## SECTION 1 — THE INSULATION PROBLEM NOBODY TALKS ABOUT

**Headline:** Insulation Without Air Sealing Is Half a Job

Here's what most homeowners don't know: insulation and air sealing are two different things, and you need both to have a properly performing building envelope.

Insulation slows the conduction of heat through walls, ceilings, and floors. But it doesn't stop air movement. Gaps around electrical boxes, plumbing penetrations, recessed light fixtures, attic hatches, and top plates allow conditioned air to move directly in and out of the building — bypassing the insulation entirely.

If you've had insulation added and still have comfort or energy problems, air leakage through unsealed penetrations is a very likely cause.

---

## SECTION 2 — WHERE AIR LEAKS HAPPEN

**Headline:** The Most Common Air Leakage Locations in Louisville Homes

- **Top plates** — The framing at the ceiling level between the living space and attic. Often has continuous gaps that allow air movement along every interior wall.
- **Attic hatch** — Frequently the worst performer in the entire building envelope. An unsealed attic hatch loses more energy per square foot than almost anything else in the house.
- **Recessed lighting** — Older can lights that penetrate into the attic are major air leakage points unless specifically sealed.
- **Plumbing and electrical penetrations** — Anywhere a pipe or wire passes through the top plate into the attic.
- **Knee walls** — In Cape Cod and story-and-a-half homes, knee wall areas are major thermal and air bypass locations.
- **Crawlspace penetrations** — Plumbing, wiring, and structural connections in crawlspaces that open into the conditioned space above.

---

## SECTION 3 — HOW WE DO AIR SEALING

**Headline:** Sealed Before the Insulation Goes In

GISCO performs air sealing as part of our residential insulation process — not as an afterthought. For attic projects, we seal top plate gaps, penetrations, and attic bypasses before the new insulation is installed. This sequence matters: trying to air seal after insulation is in place is harder and less effective.

We use foam sealants and fire-rated caulk appropriate for each application. The goal is to close the major bypass pathways so the insulation can do what it's rated to do.

## SECTION 1 — WHY PIPE INSULATION MATTERS

**Headline:** Mechanical Insulation Isn't Optional. It's Operational.

Pipe insulation on HVAC, refrigeration, chilled water, steam, and process piping systems does several things simultaneously: it controls energy loss, prevents condensation, protects personnel from contact with hot or cold surfaces, and maintains operating efficiency.

When mechanical insulation fails — through deterioration, improper installation, or inadequate specification — the consequences are immediate and measurable. Energy efficiency drops. Condensation forms on cold lines, leading to moisture damage and potential mold. Heat loss on steam or hot water systems increases fuel costs. High-temperature process lines become safety hazards.

The right insulation system, specified correctly and installed properly, prevents all of this.

---

## SECTION 2 — ELASTOMERIC INSULATION: WHAT IT IS AND WHERE IT'S USED

**Headline:** Flexible, Moisture-Resistant, and Built for Mechanical Applications

Elastomeric foam insulation (commonly known by trade names like Armaflex) is the standard for refrigeration, HVAC, and chilled water piping where moisture resistance is critical. Unlike rigid insulation that can crack under pipe movement or vibration, elastomeric is flexible, self-sealing, and inherently resistant to vapor transmission — making it the appropriate choice for cold piping applications where condensation control is essential.

GISCO installs elastomeric insulation on:
- Chilled water piping systems
- Refrigeration lines
- HVAC supply and return ductwork in unconditioned spaces
- Cold process piping in industrial applications
- Commercial building mechanical systems

---

## SECTION 3 — WHEN TO CALL US

**Headline:** Signs Your Mechanical Pipe Insulation Needs Attention

- **Visible condensation on cold lines** — Wet insulation or sweating on pipe surfaces indicates vapor barrier failure or missing insulation
- **Rising energy costs on chilled water or refrigeration systems** — May indicate insulation degradation
- **Deteriorating insulation jackets** — UV degradation, physical damage, or mastic failure on outdoor or industrial systems
- **System expansion or renovation** — New pipe runs need insulation specified and installed correctly from the start
- **Routine inspection findings** — Facilities management teams often identify insulation deficiencies during annual inspections that require a qualified contractor to address
