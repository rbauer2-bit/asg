## 3A: The Competitive Landscape

The Louisville executive and leadership coaching market presents a textbook opportunity for a new entrant with genuine credentials: a fragmented competitive field, no dominant local authority, and a target audience whose primary decision-making signal is peer recommendation and professional credibility rather than advertising volume.

Three competitive forces merit specific analysis.

**Competitor 1 — BetterUp (National Coaching Platform)**

BetterUp positions as enterprise coaching infrastructure — software-enabled, algorithmically matched, deployed at scale for large employer populations. Louisville employers including Humana, Yum! Brands, and Brown-Forman maintain active BetterUp or similar platform contracts for broad leader populations.

*What they win on:* Corporate buying relationships, brand recognition, and the ability to coach hundreds of leaders simultaneously without adding headcount to the L&D function. For an HR leader looking to check a leadership development box across a large population, a platform contract is an easy procurement decision.

*What they cannot do:* Relationship. The BetterUp model is algorithmically matched, programmatically managed, and inherently impersonal. Leaders who have used platform coaching consistently report the same experience: technically fine, personally hollow. The coach does not know the company. They do not know the culture. They do not know the specific political dynamics that are shaping the leader's challenges. Kristen's Louisville-based, relationship-first model is not a different version of what BetterUp sells. It is a different category.

*Positioning gap:* BetterUp serves populations. Kristen serves people.

**Competitor 2 — Louisville L&D and HR Consulting Firms**

A cluster of Louisville-area organizational development and HR consulting practices (including firms like OBJ & Associates, Insightful Minds Consulting, and similar regional practitioners) offer team development and leadership consulting as part of broader HR service portfolios.

*What they win on:* Established corporate relationships, the ability to bundle coaching with other HR services, and credibility within HR buying networks.

*What they cannot do:* Deep personal executive coaching. For these firms, leadership development is one service within a broad portfolio of compliance training, HR policy work, and talent administration. The coaching is a line item, not a calling. Their practitioners are generalists who know process, not specialists who know the lived experience of leading at the executive level.

*Positioning gap:* They offer programs. Kristen offers transformation.

**Competitor 3 — Individual ICF-Certified Coaches (Louisville Directory)**

A number of individual coaches are active in the Louisville market, visible on ICF directories, LinkedIn, and coach listing sites. Most hold ACC or PCC credentials and position on their certification level and coaching methodology.

*What they win on:* Credentialing recognition among buyers who are already familiar with ICF standards, and lower price points that attract budget-conscious individual buyers.

*What they cannot do:* Credibly claim 20 years of enterprise-level organizational experience. These coaches position on how they coach, not on what they know from having done the work themselves. Their content, when it exists, is largely coaching-industry content — process-heavy, theory-forward, light on organizational reality. The leaders Kristen serves have no patience for theory without application. They want someone who has been in the room.

*Positioning gap:* They are coaches. Kristen is a practitioner who coaches.

## BELIEF TRACK (What They Think Is True)

**B1 — Enemy Belief**
*"Coaching is for leaders who are struggling or failing. If I'm successful, I shouldn't need a coach. Asking for help is a signal that I've hit my ceiling — and that signal is dangerous in a corporate environment."*

The prospect at B1 is not in the market for coaching. She is actively resistant to the category. Content at this stage does not sell coaching — it interrupts the Enemy Belief by showing her a leader she respects who uses coaching as a strategic tool, not a remediation measure.

**B2 — Pre-Frame**
*"Maybe coaching isn't what I thought it was. I've seen it used in a different way — not for people who are failing, but for people who are already performing and want to accelerate. That reframe changes what I'm actually evaluating."*

The prospect is now open. The Enemy Belief has been disrupted but not replaced. Content at this stage explains the real distinction: coaching-as-remediation (the old model) vs. coaching-as-strategic-advantage (the new model). This is where the reframe is built. The goal is not to make her want coaching — it is to make her see the decision differently.

**B3 — New Worldview**
*"The leaders I admire most in organizations — the ones who seem to grow continuously, who navigate ambiguity better than everyone else, who lead teams that actually want to work for them — almost all of them have invested in some form of coaching or intentional leadership development. This isn't a crutch. It's a multiplier."*

The prospect now has a new reference frame. She is actively evaluating whether coaching could produce that same result for her. Content at this stage makes the New Worldview concrete and specific — real stories, real outcomes, real before/after comparisons that show what the investment actually produces.

**B4 — Internal Alignment**
*"I understand what coaching is and why it works. What I'm now asking is whether Kristen specifically is the right coach for me — someone who actually understands my world, my kind of organization, my kind of challenges. If she has that, I'm ready to make a decision."*

The prospect is now evaluating Kristen specifically, not coaching generically. Content at this stage is authority-building: the 20-year background, the specific language of organizational dynamics, the track record of leaders who have worked with practitioners like Kristen. This is where the corporate L&D credential closes the gap.

**B5 — Certainty**
*"She gets it. She's been inside organizations. She has seen what I'm dealing with from the inside. I know what the engagement looks like. I know what I'm investing. I know what the path forward is. I'm ready."*

The prospect has crossed the belief threshold. What remains is removing the final practical friction — ease of scheduling, confidence in the process, clear first step.

---

## EMOTION TRACK (What They Feel Is True)

**E1 — Dissatisfaction**
*"I am performing. I am delivering results. And I feel stuck in a way I cannot name. Something isn't working at the level I'm playing at now, and I have no idea what to do about it. I keep doing what got me here, and it keeps feeling less effective. I'm good at my job. Why isn't that enough anymore?"*

The emotional state at E1 is unnamed dissatisfaction — the recognition that something is off without a framework for understanding what. Content at this stage names the feeling without judgment. The prospect needs to feel seen, not coached.

**E2 — Contrast**
*"I've just been exposed to a story of a leader who was exactly where I am — the performance gap, the unclear path, the quiet frustration — and who changed that trajectory. Seeing that contrast made my current state feel more uncomfortable than it did before I saw it."*

Contrast is the emotional engine of the conversion process. A well-told story of a leader who was exactly where Amanda is now, and who changed trajectory, creates the emotional gap between current reality and possible reality. The gap creates discomfort. The discomfort creates motion.

**E3 — Desire**
*"I want what that leader has. I want to lead with that kind of confidence, that kind of clarity, that kind of team dynamic. I can see it clearly enough to want it. I'm starting to think about what it would take to get there."*

Desire is activated by making the after-state visible and specific. Not a general promise of better leadership — a specific picture of what leading with clarity and confidence looks like in the real organizational moments she faces every day: the difficult conversation she avoids, the boardroom presence she wants to build, the team dynamic she wants to shift.

**E4 — Urgency**
*"Every quarter I stay stuck in this pattern is a quarter I'm not growing. The career opportunity I'm looking at doesn't wait for me to be ready. If I delay this decision, the cost isn't zero — it's the compound interest of staying where I am while others are investing in their development."*

Urgency is surfaced by naming the real cost of inaction — not manufactured pressure, but the honest recognition that delay has a price. The leaders who invest in development today are the ones leading the organizations of tomorrow. Every quarter spent in the friction zone without intervention is a quarter of compounded opportunity cost.

**E5 — Relief**
*"I know exactly what the first step is. I know what the engagement looks like. I know what I'm investing and what I'm getting. This is not a risky or uncertain decision — it is a clear one. I'm ready to schedule the call."*

Relief removes the final emotional barrier: fear of the unknown. What happens on the consultation call? What does a coaching engagement actually feel like? What will I be asked to do? When these questions are answered before the prospect picks up the phone, the consultation converts at a dramatically higher rate.

## PRIMARY STALL POINT ANALYSIS

**Where Amanda most commonly stalls:** B1/E1 — the Enemy Belief is strong enough and the dissatisfaction is unnamed enough that she does not enter the consideration process at all. She encounters Kristen's content, feels the relevance, and then the Enemy Belief kicks in: *"But I'm not someone who needs a coach."* She closes the tab.

**The counter-move:** The most effective pattern interrupt for this stall is third-party proof — specifically, a leader Amanda respects (or a recognizable archetype she identifies with) who publicly attributes their growth to a coaching investment. The statement "I hired a coach when I was at the top of my game, not the bottom" from a credible source is worth more than 10 articles about the coaching process.

**Secondary stall point:** B3/E3 — the prospect is interested but not yet convinced Kristen specifically is the right coach. She has moved past the category skepticism and is now evaluating the practitioner. The credential that closes this gap is the 20-year corporate background — not stated as a credential but expressed through the specificity and accuracy of everything Kristen says about organizational dynamics. If she sounds like someone who has been in the room, the stall dissolves.
