# SECTION A: THE CONTENT AUTHORITY POSITION

*Vivienne Carr, CMO — Authority Systems Group™*

---

Before we assign topics, formats, or calendars — we establish the position. The position determines what Kristen publishes, what she does not publish, and what every single piece of content is measured against before it goes out.

> **Kristen LeMastus is the definitive source for Louisville's rising and established leaders on the topic of authentic leadership development — the one place where 20 years of enterprise organizational experience meets the honest, specific reality of what it takes to lead well — explained without jargon, without a program to sell, and without pretending the organizational context doesn't matter.**

This is not a content niche. It is a positioning stake. Every content piece Kristen produces either reinforces this position or dilutes it. There is no neutral.

---

## The Position Test

Before any piece of content is published, it passes this test:

**"Could a leadership development textbook, a certified coach with no organizational experience, or a content writer who has never been inside an organization have written this?"**

If yes — rewrite it until the answer is no. The entire value of Kristen's content authority position is that it is unreplicable by any competitor who does not have 20 years of doing the actual work inside organizations. That unreplicability must be visible in every piece she publishes.

The way it shows up in practice: specificity. The observation that could only come from someone who has been in the room. The pattern that only becomes visible after watching hundreds of leaders develop over years. The insight that sounds obvious once you've heard it — and that nobody else in this market is saying.

---

## What This Position Does NOT Include

The content authority position is precise. These topics sit outside it:

- General motivational leadership content ("Leadership is about people, not processes")
- Coaching-industry process content ("Here's how I structure my sessions")
- Generic career advice ("Network more, show up early, ask for feedback")
- Certification-driven credential content ("What my ICF certification means for you")
- Content that could be repurposed for any professional service firm in any market

None of the above is wrong. All of it is available everywhere. None of it builds the specific authority position that converts a Louisville executive into a coaching client.

---

## The Single-Sentence Positioning Test

When Kristen's ideal client describes her content to a colleague, they should say something like:

*"She's the coach who spent 20 years inside organizations building the programs that leaders attend — so when she writes about why leadership development doesn't stick, she's not guessing."*

If the description sounds like that — the content is on position. If it sounds like "she posts motivational leadership stuff" — the content is off position.

# SECTION B: THE FOUR CONTENT PILLARS

*Vivienne Carr, CMO — Authority Systems Group™*

---

Four pillars. Each one does a specific job on the Belief-to-Buy dual track. The allocation — 25 / 35 / 25 / 15 — is not arbitrary. It maps to where Kristen's avatar spends the most time and where the biggest conversion gaps exist.

---

## PILLAR 1 — ENEMY BELIEF DISMANTLER
**Stage pairs: B1/E1 → B2/E2 | Volume: 25% of all content**

**The job:** Open conversations that would never start otherwise. The prospect at B1/E1 is not looking for Kristen. She is actively filtering Kristen out because of the Enemy Belief: "Successful leaders don't need coaches. If I hire one, people will know I'm struggling." This pillar's job is to interrupt that filter — not by arguing with it, but by showing her a version of reality that makes the filter look like the thing that's costing her.

**The tone formula:**
*Empathy first* ("Here's why you think that — and here's why it made complete sense given everything you've been taught about leadership") → *Reframe second* ("Here's the assumption underneath that belief — and here's why it's wrong in a specific, observable way") → *New frame left open* (do not close with a pitch — leave the reframe available for her to inhabit)

**What makes Pillar 1 work for Kristen specifically:**
Kristen's 20-year insider background makes her the credible source for Enemy Belief dismantling in a way no other Louisville coach can match. She is not a theorist telling Amanda her belief is wrong. She is a practitioner who watched the belief play out across hundreds of leaders — and who can name, with specificity, what it cost the ones who held it longest.

**Formats that work for Pillar 1:**
LinkedIn text posts (the hook is everything — pattern interruption in the first line), LinkedIn short-form video (the personal face on camera moment that makes the reframe feel human rather than clinical), email subject lines designed to stop the scroll.

**The Pillar 1 content test:**
After reading this piece, does the prospect have a crack in the Enemy Belief — even a small one? Does she feel seen rather than judged? Does she want to read the next piece? If yes on all three — it passed.

---

## PILLAR 2 — AUTHORITY EDUCATOR
**Stage pairs: B2/E2 → B3/E3 | Volume: 35% of all content**

**The job:** Build the New Worldview by teaching what prospects don't know — drawn from 20 years of direct observation — and making the Before/After contrast undeniable. This is where Kristen's content becomes the standard by which her avatar measures every other piece of leadership content she encounters.

**The tone formula:**
*Teaching from direct experience* ("In 20 years of watching this pattern across hundreds of leaders, here's what I've observed...") → *Specific insight* (the thing that is not in any book, not available from any competitor, only accessible to someone who has been in the room) → *New Worldview stated clearly* ("What this means for how you think about your own development is...")

**What makes Pillar 2 work for Kristen specifically:**
The authority educator content is where Kristen's differentiation is most visible. Every observation she makes as an educator is grounded in direct organizational experience that no competitor has. The content does not sound like coaching-industry content. It sounds like someone who has watched the inside of organizations for 20 years and is finally sharing what she saw.

**Formats that work for Pillar 2:**
LinkedIn long-form articles (1,200–2,000 words), email sequences, blog posts on the authority website, guest contributions to Louisville Business First or Louisville professional publications, podcast guest appearances.

**The Pillar 2 content test:**
After reading this piece, does the prospect have a piece of knowledge or perspective she did not have before — and does it come specifically from Kristen's 20-year organizational background? Could a competitor have written this? If the answer to the last question is yes — rewrite it.

---

## PILLAR 3 — PROOF MOBILIZER
**Stage pairs: B3/E3 → B4/E4 | Volume: 25% of all content**

**The job:** Make the stakes concrete. The prospect at B3/E3 has a New Worldview — she understands coaching can be a strategic advantage. She is now evaluating Kristen specifically. This pillar answers the question: "Does she actually produce results? Does she understand people like me?" Real outcomes, told as real stories, with the emotional reality of the journey intact.

**The tone formula:**
*Lead with the before* (the specific state the client was in at the start — not "struggling" but the actual, named experience: "She had been passed over for VP and didn't understand why. Her results were strong. Her team delivered. The feedback was vague enough to be frustrating without being specific enough to act on.") → *Specific what-changed* (not "she became a better leader" but the actual observable difference: "She stopped leading the way she thought she was supposed to and started leading the way she actually thought") → *The emotional resolution* (how it felt — the part that converts, not just the outcome that impresses)

**What makes Pillar 3 work for Kristen specifically:**
Outcome stories from a coach with Kristen's background carry extra weight because the reader knows the coach has seen enough leaders to recognize a meaningful outcome from a routine one. Every story Kristen tells is implicitly backed by 20 years of comparison data.

**Compliance note:** ICF ethical standards require that outcome stories are anonymized or shared with client consent. All Pillar 3 content uses anonymized case references or, where clients have given explicit permission, named testimonials. The language convention throughout is "a leader I worked with" or "in my experience with leaders navigating this kind of challenge" — not specific identifying details without consent.

**Formats that work for Pillar 3:**
LinkedIn narrative posts (700–1,000 words — the format that LinkedIn's algorithm rewards most for dwell time), video testimonials (with client permission), case study documents for the website, email sequence anchors.

**The Pillar 3 content test:**
After reading this piece, does the prospect feel the emotional reality of the transformation — not just the outcome? Does she see herself in the before-state? Does she want that after-state for herself? If yes — it passed.

---

## PILLAR 4 — TRUST CLOSER
**Stage pairs: B4/E4 → B5/E5 | Volume: 15% of all content**

**The job:** Produce Certainty and Relief simultaneously. The prospect at B4/E4 has moved through the belief track and the emotion track. She is not unconvinced — she is uncertain about the specific: what does the engagement actually look like? What happens on the consultation call? What will she be asked? What does she invest? Every unknown at this stage is an exit. This pillar removes every unknown.

**The tone formula:**
*Maximum transparency* (say the thing that feels like too much to say — the price, the process, the what-happens-if-it-doesn't-work) → *De-risk the next step* (the consultation call is not a sales call, it is a conversation — say that specifically and describe what it actually involves) → *CTA as logical conclusion* (the next step should feel like a continuation of reading the content, not a gear shift into a sales moment)

**What makes Pillar 4 work for Kristen specifically:**
The discovery call with no pitch is Kristen's most powerful Trust Closer asset. A coach who will spend 45 minutes assessing honest fit — and who will say "I don't think coaching is the right tool for this challenge right now" when that's the truth — signals something most coaches don't. That signal, named directly in Pillar 4 content, closes the remaining trust gap.

**Formats that work for Pillar 4:**
Website service pages, FAQ sections, LinkedIn posts specifically about the consultation process, email sequence closers (Email 5–6 of Welcome Sequence and Belief-to-Buy Sequence), Google Business profile posts.

**The Pillar 4 content test:**
After reading this piece, does the prospect know exactly what the first step looks like — with no remaining uncertainty about the process? Does the CTA feel like the next logical step rather than a pitch? If yes — it passed.

# SECTION F: BRAND VOICE APPLICATION GUIDE

*Vivienne Carr, CMO — Authority Systems Group™*

---

Kristen's brand voice — warm, direct, high-conviction, outcomes-oriented — applies differently to each content pillar. The guide below maps the voice to the specific writing approach required for each pillar type.

---

## Pillar 1: Enemy Belief Dismantler — Voice Guide

**The tone:** Warm first. Challenging second. Never superior.

The Enemy Belief content fails when it makes the prospect feel judged for holding the belief. She held it because every message she has ever received about leadership told her to. The tone opens with genuine validation of where the belief comes from — and then shifts to reframe with the authority of someone who has watched its consequences play out at scale.

**Opening constructions that work:**
- "There is a belief I encounter in almost every first conversation with a senior leader..."
- "I held a version of this belief myself for a long time..."
- "After 20 years of watching this pattern, here is what I know..."

**Opening constructions that don't work:**
- "Most people think... but they're wrong." (superior, judgmental)
- "The biggest mistake leaders make is..." (shame-triggering)
- "If you believe X, you're leaving money on the table." (transactional, premature)

**The Kristen-specific voice test for Pillar 1:**
Does the opening feel like it was written by someone who genuinely understands why the prospect holds this belief — because she has watched it protect leaders from real risk inside real organizations for 20 years? If yes — on voice. If it feels like it's lecturing — rewrite.

---

## Pillar 2: Authority Educator — Voice Guide

**The tone:** Teaching from direct experience. Specific. Never theoretical.

The authority educator content is where Kristen's 20-year insider background is most visible. The voice is the practitioner who has been in the room, not the researcher who has read about it. Every claim is grounded in direct observation — "I have watched this pattern across hundreds of leaders" not "research suggests."

**Opening constructions that work:**
- "In 20 years of building leadership programs inside organizations, I've watched this pattern..."
- "Here is what I observed across dozens of organizations about [topic]..."
- "The leaders I watched develop the fastest had one thing in common that the programs didn't teach..."

**The insider specificity test:**
After reading the piece, could the reader identify one specific, concrete observation that could only come from someone who has been inside organizations? If the piece could have been written from a book — it failed the test.

**What to avoid:**
- Coaching-industry jargon without plain-English translation ("somatic awareness," "liminal space," "co-creating the container")
- References to research without grounding in what Kristen personally observed that confirms or complicates the research
- Generic leadership truisms that are true everywhere and specific nowhere ("Leadership is about relationships," "Feedback is a gift")

---

## Pillar 3: Proof Mobilizer — Voice Guide

**The tone:** Story-first. Specific. Emotionally honest without oversharing.

The proof content lives or dies on the specificity of the before-state. "A leader who was struggling" does not create identification. "A Director with 12 years at her company, strong performance reviews, and a passed-over VP promotion she didn't understand" creates identification. Kristen's voice in proof content is the practitioner who saw the full picture — who knows the specific emotional texture of the before-state because she was in the room.

**The story arc:**
1. **Before:** Name the specific emotional and situational reality. Not what went wrong — what it felt like to carry what wasn't working while looking like everything was fine.
2. **The work:** What specifically was examined, challenged, or addressed. Not the full play-by-play — the one or two things that unlocked the change.
3. **The shift:** Not "she became a better leader." The specific, observable moment when something was different — in a meeting, a conversation, a feedback exchange.
4. **The emotional resolution:** How she felt after — not just what she achieved.

**What to avoid:**
- Outcome language that sounds like a before/after ad ("She 10x'd her leadership effectiveness")
- Specific identifying details without client consent
- ROI claims without qualification ("results vary based on engagement, commitment, and context")

---

## Pillar 4: Trust Closer — Voice Guide

**The tone:** Maximum transparency. Process-forward. De-risking without over-explaining.

Trust Closer content says the things that feel like too much to say — the price, the process, what happens if it doesn't work, why the discovery call is not a sales call. The voice is a practitioner who is confident enough in the quality of her work to be completely transparent about what it involves and what it costs.

**What maximum transparency sounds like:**
- "Here is exactly what happens in the first 45 minutes. Here are the questions I will ask. Here is what I will tell you if I don't think coaching is the right tool for your challenge right now."
- "Executive coaching engagements start at $2,500/month. Individual coaching starts at $1,500/month. There are no hidden fees."
- "I will be honest with you if this isn't the right fit. I would rather have that conversation before we start than after you've invested in something that isn't serving you."

**What to avoid:**
- Manufactured urgency ("spots are limited")
- Vague process descriptions that leave the prospect with more questions than she started with
- Sales language disguised as transparency ("I'm excited to share that...")

## THE INPUT MODEL

Every piece of content Kristen publishes draws on source material that only she can provide: the specific observations, the real stories (anonymized), the practitioner insights that are grounded in 20 years of organizational work. The content strategy works because the source material is unreplicable — but the source material only exists in Kristen's head until she shares it.

**The 20-minute weekly input:**
Once per week, Kristen records a 15–20 minute voice memo covering:
1. One observation, pattern, or story from the week's client work or the 20-year experience bank
2. One thing a client said this week that named their challenge in a way that felt exactly right
3. Any news, event, or professional community development she's aware of that could be a content angle

This voice memo is submitted by Sunday at noon. ASG produces two LinkedIn post drafts for that week from the source material. Kristen reviews and approves (or requests one revision round) by Tuesday noon. Posts are scheduled for Monday and Thursday of the following week.

**The monthly anchor review:**
On the first Monday of each month, Kristen receives the outline for that month's long-form article. The outline is based on the prior month's post engagement data — which topics generated the most comments, saves, and DMs becomes the anchor piece direction. Kristen approves or adjusts the outline within 48 hours. ASG produces the full draft within 5 business days of outline approval.

## CONTENT QUALITY GATE

Every piece of content that leaves ASG passes Vivienne Carr's three-question quality check before entering Kristen's approval queue:

1. **The Position Test:** Does this piece reinforce Kristen's authority position — "20 years inside organizations, now coaching from the outside" — in a specific and visible way? Could a competitor with no organizational background have written this?

2. **The Stage Test:** Does this piece target a specific stage pair on the Belief-to-Buy dual track? Is it doing a specific job — or is it general awareness content with no measurable conversion function?

3. **The Voice Test:** Does this sound like Kristen — warm, direct, specific, practitioner-grounded, outcomes-focused — and not like a coaching-industry template? Would her network recognize this as hers?

If any answer is no — the piece is rewritten before it goes to Kristen's queue.
