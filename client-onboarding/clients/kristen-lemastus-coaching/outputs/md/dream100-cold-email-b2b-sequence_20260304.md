## SECTION A: INDUSTRY DOMINANCE THROUGH RESEARCH

### 1. Size of Market

The global coaching industry generates approximately **$4.56 billion annually** (ICF Global Coaching Study, 2023), with the United States accounting for the largest single-market share — estimated at **$1.9–$2.1 billion per year**. The number of active coaches globally surpassed **109,000** as of 2023, with the U.S. housing the highest density.

Executive coaching — the segment Kristen occupies — commands the highest average fee rates in the category: **$500–$3,500 per month** for individual engagements, and **$10,000–$50,000** for organizational team development engagements. At the Louisville metro level, the market is effectively untracked — coaching engagements are rarely reported in local business data, but the corporate ecosystem (Humana, Yum! Brands, Brown-Forman, Norton Healthcare, Republic Bank, plus a dense mid-market manufacturing and financial services sector) generates substantial annual demand. Estimated Louisville metro addressable market for executive and leadership coaching: **$8–$14 million annually across all providers**, individual and organizational.

### 2. Major Trends

**2019–2026 pattern: Coaching demand has accelerated, not contracted, through economic uncertainty.** Post-pandemic data from the International Coaching Federation shows a **54% increase in demand** for executive and leadership coaching between 2020 and 2023 — driven specifically by the leadership identity disruptions of hybrid work, rapid promotion cycles during talent wars, and the elevated visible failure rate of leaders who were technically competent but organizationally underprepared.

**The credentialing saturation problem:** ICF-certified coaches have grown from approximately 53,000 globally in 2019 to over 109,000 in 2023 — a 106% increase in four years. The supply of certified coaches is growing faster than the market's ability to differentiate between them. This creates a specific advantage for practitioners who can claim something that certification cannot confer: documented organizational experience. In a market of 109,000 coaches all holding credentials, the coach who spent 20 years building programs inside organizations stands apart not because of a credential but because of a career.

**LinkedIn is the discovery channel for B2B coaching.** A 2024 survey of corporate buyers of coaching services found that **73% begin their vendor evaluation process on LinkedIn** — specifically looking for thought leadership content that demonstrates subject-matter expertise. The Louisville executive coaching market is essentially invisible on LinkedIn at the practitioner level. No Louisville-based solo coach has established consistent, quality thought leadership. The content authority position is unoccupied.

**Organizations are the fastest-growing coaching buyer segment.** Organizational buyers now account for **49% of all coaching revenue** (up from 36% in 2019). HR Directors, CHROs, and L&D leaders at mid-market companies are increasingly bringing external coaching practitioners into their organizations rather than relying solely on platform contracts. This creates an expanding organizational revenue stream adjacent to individual executive coaching.

### 3. Current Industry Problems ("Bad News")

**Problem 1 — The Promotion Gap:** Gallup research documents that 82% of new managers are in over their heads at their current leadership level. Leaders are promoted for technical performance — and then expected to lead teams, manage culture, and develop other people without any preparation for the entirely different skill set leadership requires. The organization that promoted them rarely provides the support to bridge the gap. The result is the predictable failure pattern Kristen's entire career has been spent addressing.

**Problem 2 — The L&D Investment Black Hole:** McKinsey research consistently finds that **70% of organizational L&D investment produces no measurable behavior change**. The reason: programs deliver information without the individual accountability structure that produces sustained application. Workshops teach frameworks that disappear by Monday morning because there is no coach following up on what changed and why. This makes Kristen's individual coaching model — with its between-session availability and session-by-session accountability — the specific solution to the biggest known failure mode in the market.

**Problem 3 — The Coaching Signal Problem:** The #1 reason high-performing leaders don't hire coaches is documented and consistent: **"I should be able to figure it out myself."** (ICF Global Coaching Study, 2023.) The belief that successful leaders don't need coaches — that coaching signals struggle rather than ambition — is the primary barrier to market conversion. This is the Enemy Belief that Kristen's entire content architecture is designed to dismantle.

**Problem 4 — The Generic Coach Problem:** In a market of 109,000 certified coaches, the default positioning is identical: "I help leaders unlock their potential." This phrase, and variations of it, appear on the websites of roughly 80% of practicing coaches. The result is a market where buyers cannot distinguish between practitioners, default to price comparison, and deprioritize the investment entirely. Practitioners who can claim a specific, unreplicable positioning — Kristen's 20-year enterprise L&D background — break out of this commoditization trap.

**Problem 5 — The Remote Coach Limitations:** National coaching platforms (BetterUp, CoachHub) serve populations at scale but cannot serve individual leaders with organizational specificity. A coach who does not know Louisville's corporate culture — who does not understand the specific dynamics of leading inside Humana's healthcare infrastructure, or Brown-Forman's family-owned culture, or the mid-market entrepreneurial texture of Louisville's manufacturing sector — cannot provide the specific insight that a local practitioner brings. The local-practitioner premium is real and currently unclaimed.

### 4. Custom Niche-Specific Insights

**The Louisville corporate leadership ecosystem is unusually dense for a metro of its size.** Two Fortune 500 headquarters (Humana, Kindred Healthcare), Yum! Brands (Louisville-based, 36,000 employees globally), Brown-Forman (Louisville-based, 4,700 employees), Norton Healthcare (Louisville-based, 20,000 employees), Baptist Health (Louisville-based, 23,000 employees), Republic Bank, LG&E and KU Energy — plus the largest concentration of privately held bourbon industry companies in the world — create a corporate professional population that significantly exceeds what a typical 800,000-person metropolitan area produces. Louisville punches above its weight class as a coaching market.

**Leadership Louisville Center places 100+ senior leaders through flagship programs annually.** This represents both a talent development ecosystem and a community that Kristen's authority position should penetrate early — alumni of the Leadership Louisville programs are precisely the Ascending Amanda avatar who already values leadership development and is primed for individual coaching.

**The bourbon industry is a specific niche expansion opportunity.** Brown-Forman, Heaven Hill, Buffalo Trace, Beam Suntory (Clermont, KY) — the senior leadership layer of Louisville's signature industry is a concentrated, relationship-driven community where local presence and word-of-mouth authority carry disproportionate weight. A single organizational engagement with a bourbon industry company produces referral relationships throughout the sector.

## SECTION B: PAIN POINTS AND BUYER MOTIVATION

### Five Pain Points That Grab Attention (Pre-Sale Awareness)

1. **The Plateau Nobody Names:** High performance at one level doesn't guarantee high performance at the next. The leader who made VP on the strength of her execution skills is now expected to develop other people, build culture, and lead through ambiguity — using skills she was never taught. She knows something isn't working. She doesn't have language for what it is.

2. **The 360 That Changed Everything:** A 360 review named a leadership pattern she has never seen in herself. The feedback is specific, attributable to real observations, and impossible to dismiss. She does not know what to do with it — and she does not have anyone in her organization she can be fully honest with about what it landed.

3. **The Team She Can't Fix:** Her team is underperforming and she can't determine if the problem is the team or her leadership. She has tried harder, communicated more clearly, and restructured the work — and the results haven't moved. The possibility that she is part of the problem is uncomfortable and unexamined.

4. **The Peer Who Is Accelerating:** Someone in her peer cohort — same tenure, same track record, broadly equivalent capability — is visibly growing faster than she is. She has heard they hired a coach. She has dismissed this twice as a coincidence. It is not a coincidence.

5. **The Quiet Exhaustion:** She is leading others and nobody is leading her. She gives feedback, holds her team accountable, develops her people — and the reciprocal investment in her own development is absent. The loneliness of senior leadership is real and it is wearing on her in ways she rarely admits out loud.

### Five Pain Points That Motivate Faster Buying

1. **The Promotion Window Is Open — Now:** A senior role is visible on the organizational horizon. The leaders who are being positioned for it are known to her. The decision window is 6–12 months. She knows she needs to be different by the time that decision is made, not after it.

2. **The Feedback Is Fresh:** A recent performance review, 360, or direct conversation named something specific. The discomfort is present-tense, not abstract. The moment when feedback lands is the highest-receptivity moment for coaching — the window closes as the discomfort normalizes.

3. **The Peer's Results Are Visible:** She has now observed, over 60–90 days, that the colleague who started coaching is different in specific ways she can name. The results are no longer theoretical.

4. **The New Role Has Started:** She just moved into a new leadership position — new scope, new team, new expectations. The first 90 days are the highest-stakes, highest-development period of any new role. She knows she should be investing in getting it right, not just getting through it.

5. **The Year-End Reflection:** It is Q4 or the new year. She is assessing honestly where she is as a leader relative to where she expected to be 12 months ago. The gap is visible in a way it isn't during the grind of the working year. January is the highest-inquiry month for individual coaching precisely because this reflection produces urgency.

### Five Pain Points That Motivate Buyers to Buy More

1. **The Team Emerges as a Development Need:** Within 6 months of individual coaching, she has the clarity to see that her team has specific development gaps she could address at an organizational level — and she refers Kristen to her CHRO or COO.

2. **The New Level Requires New Work:** She achieves the goals she came in with — and then discovers that the next level of leadership requires a new set of development. The engagement continues not because of inertia but because the work compounds.

3. **She Refers Her Peers:** The coaching results produce visible change that her peers notice. She becomes the person who refers. Two to three introductions within 12 months of signing is the documented pattern for satisfied executive coaching clients.

4. **The Organization Wants to Scale It:** A satisfied individual client who is now a VP or C-suite leader initiates conversations about organizational programs — team development, culture consulting, or leadership cohort engagements. The individual engagement becomes the organizational pipeline.

5. **The Career Transition Activates a New Cycle:** She takes a new role, moves to a new company, or steps into a larger scope. Each major career transition is a new activation moment — the investment that prepared her for the transition creates the relationship she calls on when the next one arrives.

## SECTION C: DEEP DESIRES + FUTURE STATE

### Five Emotional Benefits Clients Get

1. **The End of Performance:** She stops leading the way she was told she should lead and starts leading the way she actually thinks. The relief of being herself in the boardroom — not a modified version of what she thought was expected — is the benefit her peers can see without knowing what to name.

2. **The Specific Language She's Been Missing:** She has been watching a pattern in herself or her team for months and hasn't been able to name it. Coaching gives her the precise language to see it clearly — and naming it is 60% of addressing it. This is what 20 years of watching patterns inside organizations produces that textbooks do not.

3. **A Witness to the Whole Picture:** Someone who knows her full context — the political reality of her organization, the specific team dynamic she's managing, the gap between her public leadership and her private experience of it — holds that entire picture session after session. She does not have to re-explain herself. She does not have to be careful about what she says. The relief of being fully known in the context of her work is profound.

4. **Feedback That Energizes:** Every performance review in her career has told her what to improve. Coaching tells her how she is growing, names the change she cannot see in herself from the inside, and invests in her future — not her remediation. The emotional experience of feedback that is genuinely for her is different from any other feedback she has received in her professional life.

5. **The Specific Tool for Monday Morning:** She does not leave sessions with good ideas. She leaves with a specific, tested tool for the conversation she has been avoiding, the team dynamic she has been managing around, or the leadership behavior she has been performing. The specificity is the benefit — the tool works in her actual organization, with her actual team, in the real political context she operates in.

### Five "Future-Paced" Benefits

1. **The Team Dynamic She Always Wanted to Build:** Eighteen months from now, she is leading a team that senior leaders point to as the example. Not because she worked harder — because she led differently. The team dynamic she always believed was possible, built by the leader she has become, is the compounding return on the coaching investment.

2. **The Boardroom Presence She Has Been Building Toward:** She walks into a high-stakes presentation and the outcome feels settled before it starts. Not because she is performing confidence — because she has it. The clarity about her leadership identity, built over months of focused development, has changed how she carries herself in the rooms that matter most.

3. **The Call About the Senior Role:** The conversation she has been hoping for happens because the right people in her organization have seen, over the past 12 months, a different version of her. Not a harder version. A clearer, more deliberately intentional, more distinctly hers version. The call is a recognition of who she has become.

4. **The Moment She Says to a Direct Report What She Always Wished Someone Had Said to Her:** She is giving feedback to a high-potential leader on her team and she realizes, mid-conversation, that she is saying exactly what she needed to hear at that stage of her career. The compounding legacy of leadership development — she is now building others the way she was built.

5. **Looking Back Without Recognizing the Before:** Eighteen months from now, she cannot easily remember what it felt like to carry the weight of her leadership alone, to not have language for the patterns she was living inside, to not know what to do with the feedback that named a gap she didn't understand. The distance is that complete.

Subject: why the best leaders I've worked with stopped asking "am I good enough"
Subject B: what I noticed about leaders who grow continuously (vs. those who don't)

---

Something I've observed over nearly 20 years of working inside organizations:

The leaders who develop continuously — the ones whose teams want to work for them, whose peers rely on them, who are invited into the rooms that matter — almost never talk about their development in terms of fixing something. They talk about it in terms of becoming someone. The conversation isn't "I got feedback and now I need to address it." It's "I have a clear picture of the leader I'm trying to become, and I'm investing in the gap between where I am and where I want to be."

The leaders who stall — and I've watched this pattern play out across dozens of organizations and hundreds of leadership programs — tend to do the opposite. They evaluate the need for development against what's visibly broken. If nothing is visibly broken, there is no development need. They are waiting for a problem serious enough to justify the investment.

Here is what I noticed about the difference between those two groups over time:

The first group rarely had a worse starting point. They didn't have more resources, more time, or more organizational support. They had a different relationship with the idea of growth itself. They had decoupled "needing to develop" from "there is something wrong with me." And that single reframe — development as strategic investment, not remediation — produced compounding results across their careers that the second group consistently underperformed.

I have spent 20 years watching this pattern. And it is the reason I now coach executives and leaders who are already performing well — not leaders who have hit bottom.

Something to sit with.

Subject: two types of leaders I've watched develop over 20 years
Subject B: the development pattern that separates good leaders from great ones

---

In 20 years of building leadership programs and watching thousands of leaders move through them, I've observed two distinct approaches to professional development. Not two types of people — two patterns of decision-making that anyone can move between.

I'll describe them plainly, without judgment.

**The first type** treats development as a response to signal. Something has to go visibly wrong — a piece of feedback, a missed promotion, a team performance problem, a 360 result — before the investment is justified. Until the signal arrives, the logic is: *I'm performing. There's no evidence of a problem. The investment doesn't have a clear enough return.*

What this type typically experiences over time: development happens in episodic bursts, driven by external events. Growth is reactive. The pattern of leadership stays largely stable between events. The ceiling is defined by what circumstances reveal, not by what the leader chooses to build.

**The second type** treats development as a continuous, proactive investment — not because anything is broken, but because they have a clear picture of the leader they're building toward and they are actively closing the gap. The signal for investment isn't a problem. It's the distance between where they are and where they're trying to go.

What this type typically experiences over time: growth compounds. Each investment produces a slightly different leader who is then capable of a slightly higher level of development. The ceiling expands because the leader is choosing to expand it — not waiting for circumstances to force the expansion.

The downstream consequences of each pattern are not immediate. They are visible at the 3–5 year mark.

I'm not recommending either one. I'm describing what I've watched.

Subject: the cost of waiting for the signal
Subject B: why reactive development has a price that shows up years later

---

In the organizations I've worked inside, there is a predictable pattern that plays out at the VP and Director level — and I want to name it directly, because it's rarely discussed with the honesty it deserves.

A leader performs strongly at one level. She is promoted. The new level requires a different set of capabilities — not harder technical work, but a different kind of leadership: building culture, developing others, influencing without authority, navigating the political reality of a larger organizational stage. Nobody prepares her for this transition explicitly. The assumption — from the organization and often from the leader herself — is that the skills that produced the promotion will produce success at the next level.

Sometimes they do. Often, they don't.

What happens next is the tradeoff I want to name: the leader waits for the signal. She keeps doing what worked before. She manages performance instead of building leadership. She addresses the visible problems without examining whether her own patterns are part of what's creating them. She waits until something goes visibly wrong — a 360, a missed opportunity, a team that is performing below expectations — before she treats the gap as something worth addressing.

The tradeoff is this: by waiting for the signal, she has transferred control of her development timeline to external events. The signal arrives on its own schedule. It is often inconvenient. And it arrives with urgency attached — urgency that makes it harder to approach calmly, harder to address with the kind of sustained investment that produces real change, and harder to keep from looking like remediation rather than development.

The alternative is investing before the signal. Proactively. On a timeline the leader controls. With the clarity to address patterns before they become visible problems.

I have watched both approaches play out, at scale, across 20 years of organizational work.

The leaders who invested proactively are, consistently, the ones the organization relies on at the next crisis. The leaders who waited for the signal addressed the crisis — and then often found that the next signal arrived sooner than expected.

I'm not blaming. The reactive approach is what the culture of most organizations rewards. I'm just describing the tradeoff.

Subject: what executive coaching actually involves (it's not what most people think)
Subject B: why the leaders I've watched get the most from coaching are already performing well

---

I want to be specific about what executive coaching actually involves — because the gap between what most leaders assume it is and what it actually does is wide enough to explain most of the hesitation I encounter.

The assumption I hear most often: coaching is a structured program of conversations where a trained professional helps you address a specific problem — usually a leadership gap, a performance issue, or a behavior that feedback has identified. The coach has a methodology. You follow it. You address the problem.

That is one version of coaching. It is the remediation model. And it is the version that produced the belief — documented in research, confirmed in thousands of conversations — that "successful leaders don't need coaches."

Here is what executive coaching at its most effective actually involves:

**A consistent, honest relationship with someone who knows your full context.** Not just what you tell them in session — the history, the team dynamic, the specific political landscape you're navigating, the gap between the leader you present in public and the leader you experience from the inside. This relationship produces a kind of insight that no book, program, or peer conversation can replicate: someone who knows your specific situation and can see the patterns you've become too familiar with to notice.

**Between-session continuity.** The coaching conversation doesn't produce lasting change. The experiment you run between sessions — the specific, agreed-upon behavior you try in the real organizational moment — is where the change happens. Coaching that only operates in the hour of the session is worth a fraction of coaching that provides the accountability structure to apply the insight.

**An agenda that belongs to the client, not the coach.** The most effective coaching engagement is not a program the coach runs you through. It is a standing relationship where the client brings the actual challenges — the conversation she's avoiding, the team dynamic that isn't resolving, the leadership moment she wants to handle differently next time — and the coach provides the outside perspective, the specific tool, and the accountability to do something different.

Where most leaders go wrong: they evaluate coaching based on the remediation model. They ask, "Is something wrong enough to justify this?" The right question is, "Is there a gap between the leader I am now and the leader I want to be in 18 months — and would a consistent, honest relationship with someone who has been inside organizations accelerate that journey?"

That question has a different answer.
