### When You're Ready to Understand Your Legal Options

**A resource for women in Northern Kentucky navigating separation or divorce**

---

**What most people don't know before they call an attorney:**

You don't have to be ready to file to call a lawyer. A consultation is simply a conversation about your situation and your options. Nothing is set in motion until you decide it is.

You also don't have to choose between protecting yourself and keeping things civil. A collaborative approach means both are possible.

---

**What collaborative divorce means — in plain language:**

Collaborative divorce is a process designed to resolve your case with minimum court involvement, minimum conflict, and maximum control over the outcome. Instead of each side positioning for battle, both parties work with their attorneys toward a negotiated agreement that addresses property, finances, and — if children are involved — a parenting plan built around their stability.

It doesn't mean giving up what's fair. It means pursuing what's fair without turning your family into a casualty.

---

**Common questions before a first call:**

*"I'm not sure I'm ready to file. Is it too early to talk to an attorney?"*
No. In fact, understanding your options before you're ready to act puts you in a much stronger position. A consultation is confidential and doesn't commit you to anything.

*"I'm worried about what this will cost."*
We understand. We offer flexible payment arrangements, and we'll give you a straightforward picture of what your situation is likely to require. No billing surprises.

*"I'm afraid calling an attorney will make things worse."*
It depends entirely on the attorney. An attorney who leads with conflict creates conflict. We lead with the outcome you actually want.

*"My spouse hasn't gotten a lawyer yet. Should I wait?"*
Consulting an attorney is not a hostile act. Understanding your rights protects you. Waiting until the other side has legal representation does not.

## THERAPIST FOLLOW-UP PROTOCOL

After the initial outreach:

**Day 7** (no response): Brief follow-up email. Subject: "Following up — Melissa Doss." Two sentences: "I wanted to follow up on the letter I sent last week. If a brief call makes sense, I'm happy to work around your schedule."

**Day 14** (no response): No third contact on the same thread. Move to next batch.

**After a conversation or meeting**: Send a thank-you note within 24 hours. Handwritten preferred. Reiterate your approach. Leave the door open.

**When a therapist makes a referral**: Call or email personally within the same business day to acknowledge it and confirm you're following up with their client. Keep the therapist informed on whether contact was made (without disclosing confidential information). This is what builds a real referral relationship.

## STRATEGY NOTES
*Vivienne Carr, CMO | Authority Systems Group™*

**Why this works**: Therapists refer to attorneys regularly but rarely have a trusted relationship. Most referrals are default or reluctant. Melissa's collaborative approach gives therapists a genuine reason to recommend her specifically — she is the opposite of what therapists fear an attorney will do to their client's emotional stability.

**The co-branded resource is the anchor**: It gives therapists something to hand a client who isn't ready to call an attorney yet. It keeps Melissa's name in the room during the consideration phase.

**Positioning note**: The outreach letter deliberately does not pitch. It establishes alignment of values and asks for a conversation. Therapists receive many vendor pitches and ignore them. A peer-to-peer professional introduction is different.
