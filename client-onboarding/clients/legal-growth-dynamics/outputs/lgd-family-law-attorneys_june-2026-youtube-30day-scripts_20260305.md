# PART 1: MONTHLY STRATEGY OVERVIEW

## The Mirror Architecture: June Theme Selection → YouTube Script Pack

The June 2026 YouTube pack mirrors the June theme selection calendar — topic-for-topic, day-for-day, stage-for-stage, across 21 business days (June 19, Juneteenth, carries a social post only; no YouTube script produced per content instruction in the theme selection).

June presents a structurally different challenge from April and May. April named the problem. May closed the urgency window. June opens the summer — which most family law managing partners treat as a coast, not a compound. The entire June content arc is designed to reframe that instinct. "Own the Summer" is not a seasonal headline. It's an operating philosophy: the attorneys who treat June and July as infrastructure months will enter September with a running system. The ones who coast will start from scratch when case volume comes back.

**The dual-audience problem — and how June solves it:**

By June, LGD's content has been in market for four months. A segment of the audience has watched the full April and May arc — they understand pipeline ownership in principle, they've heard the urgency case, and they're somewhere between "considering" and "committed." These are the warm-audience viewers. June's job with them: maintain Stage 5 energy, reframe summer as opportunity instead of drift, and hold the door open for the decision that May didn't fully close.

The cold audience entering June for the first time sees none of that history. They encounter a fresh Stage 2 problem frame in Week 1, move through Stages 3 and 4 across Weeks 2–4, and arrive at a Stage 4→5 bridge by the Week 5 close. The content is written so both audiences receive what they need from the same piece.

The YouTube video is the proof behind the frame. The LinkedIn post names the frame; the video earns the belief. A managing partner who reads the June 8 LinkedIn post about the content ocean will watch the corresponding YouTube video and come away with a complete mental model of how content authority distributes reach at scale — without the attorney managing every relationship manually. That's belief-level movement. That's what eleven minutes of specific, niche-exact content produces that a post never can.

## Avatar Psychology: Why Long-Form Works for Managing Partner Matt in June

June is where skepticism softens or hardens. The managing partner who has been watching LGD content since April is either moving toward a decision or retreating into "I'll revisit this after summer." Long-form video in June does something short-form can't: it gives him twelve minutes to be alone with the argument.

He is not going to watch a 10-minute YouTube video at his desk between client calls. He will watch it at 6:45am before anyone else is in the office, or on Saturday morning before his family wakes up. This is a content format for the quiet window — and June, for family law attorneys, is full of quiet windows. Intake is slower. The contested matters are in process. The calendar has room. The attorney who has always meant to "figure out the marketing thing" has no better month to actually think about it than June.

Long-form positions LGD not as a vendor selling a service but as a partner demonstrating deep knowledge of the attorney's world. By minute eight of a well-built video, the managing partner isn't evaluating LGD anymore. He's absorbing the framework. That absorption is conversion — just not the kind that requires a click.

The Homeownership Month frame (pipeline ownership vs. pipeline renting) is June's primary metaphor throughout all five weeks. It's legible to Managing Partner Matt immediately because he understands equity. He built equity in his home. He has almost certainly never applied that mental model to his practice's pipeline. June is the month that installs it.

## SECTION 2 — SETUP / PROBLEM FRAME (0:45–2:00)

Here's the thing about being generic in family law marketing. It doesn't feel like a mistake when you're doing it. It feels like prudence.

The reasoning goes like this: "If I say I only work with business owners, I'll miss the people going through a regular divorce." Or: "If I specialize in custody disputes, I'll lose the asset division cases." Or the most common one: "My referral sources send me everything, and I don't want to narrow what they think I can handle."

I understand that logic. It's not wrong on its face. And it is quietly costing family law managing partners more than almost any other decision they make about their practice.

[B-ROLL: A Google search results page for "family law attorney [city]." Eight listings. Every one of them has the same structure: name, "family law attorney," a star rating. Nothing that distinguishes any of them from the next. The visual of perfect, expensive sameness.]

Here's what happens when you're generic. A CPA has a business-owner client going through a divorce. The business has real complexity — two entities, a buy-sell agreement, a partner who's about to become an adversary. The CPA calls the attorney they know. Not the most technically skilled attorney. Not the most experienced. The one who came to mind immediately when they thought "business owner divorce." Because that attorney has, at some point, made it legible that this is their specific zone of mastery.

You may be ten times more capable of handling that case. If you haven't made your specific mastery visible — if your website, your LinkedIn, your content all say "family law attorney" and nothing more — you are not the attorney that CPA calls.

[B-ROLL: A split screen. Left: a generic attorney bio — practice areas listed, headshot, years of experience. This is not a marketing problem. It's a positioning problem. And positioning is not something that happens to you — it's something you build, deliberately, through the content you put into the world about what you actually do at your highest level.

The attorneys who have figured this out are not more talented than you. They're just more willing to be specific in public.

## CONTRARIAN VARIANT — VIDEO 1

**Opens by challenging a conventional assumption the avatar holds.**

---

Everyone in legal marketing tells you to make your website more welcoming, your content more approachable, your brand more human. And that's fine advice as far as it goes. Here's what they don't tell you: being more approachable makes you more forgettable.

The referral economy in family law doesn't run on warmth. It runs on specificity. And the attorneys with the warmest, most approachable, most "full-service" positioning are, in many markets, the ones getting the fewest inbound referrals for the cases they actually want.

I want to challenge an assumption you may be carrying: the idea that being specific about what you do best will shrink your referral base. The fear is that if you claim a specialty, the referral sources who send general cases will stop sending them.

## SECTION 4 — THE PROOF / MECHANISM (3:30–7:00)

[CHAPTER: The Client-Frame System — ~3:30]

The system I use is called the Client-Frame System. Three questions, applied before you film every video. Not after. Before.

**Question 1: Who is in the room right now?**

Not your general audience. Not your niche. The specific person. Right now. What are they sitting with? What did they search to find you? What are they afraid to ask anyone in their life? What did they type into YouTube at 11pm hoping someone would just tell them the truth?

**Question 2: What does this person need to believe before they can take the next step?**

[CHAPTER: The Belief Target — ~4:45]

**Question 3: What's the one thing they should do differently because they watched this?**

[CHAPTER: The Action Bridge — ~5:45]

Not a long list of takeaways. One thing. Concrete. Tied to their situation, not yours. The action bridge is what separates educational content from authority content.

[B-ROLL: Attorney ending a video with a specific, situational instruction — "This week, write down the three most pressing questions you'd want answered in a consultation. Keep the list. That list is your map." Then: client doing exactly that, feeling productive, feeling forward momentum.]

[PACING: deliberate — this is the payoff section, let each point land]

Educational content says "here's what you need to know." Authority content says "here's what you should do right now, given your situation, and here's why it matters." The action bridge is the difference.

When you run the Client-Frame System — who's in the room, what belief needs to shift, what one action follows — your video stops being a performance of your expertise and becomes a guide for their next move. And people who feel guided don't shop around. They call.
