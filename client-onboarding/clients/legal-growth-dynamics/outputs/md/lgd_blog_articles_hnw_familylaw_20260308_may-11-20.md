## Article 11: What Happens to Your Practice When Google Changes Its Algorithm

**Hurdle:** 7 — Google Dependency & Fragile Lead Sources
**ACP Stage Pair:** B1/E1 — Enemy Belief / Dissatisfaction
**Angle:** Risk awareness — the brittleness of single-channel lead dependency
**Hook Type:** Concrete event — this has already happened to practices like yours
**Word Count Target:** 450–550

---

May is Mental Health Awareness Month — a month dedicated to naming the invisible stressors that affect performance, decision-making, and wellbeing. Here's one that most family law attorneys carry but rarely name out loud: the anxiety of a practice that depends on a platform they don't control.

In August 2023, Google rolled out a major Helpful Content Update. Thousands of law firm websites lost significant organic ranking in a matter of days. Some dropped off page one entirely. Phones slowed. Intake dropped. And there was nothing those attorneys could do about it — because they'd built their entire lead architecture on a foundation that belongs to someone else.

This is not a hypothetical. It happened. It will happen again.

### The Platform Dependency Problem

Google is an excellent lead channel. It can generate highly intent-qualified prospects who are actively looking for a family law attorney in your market.

But Google is not a reliable one. It is a platform with its own business objectives, its own algorithm, and its own update schedule — none of which take your intake pipeline into account.

Practices that have built their lead generation primarily or exclusively around organic search rankings or Google Ads are making a structural bet: that Google's rules won't change in a way that disadvantages them, and that their budget will always be sufficient to maintain visibility in a rising-cost auction market.

Both of those bets have lost for practices that made them.

### The Visibility Illusion

Being on page one of Google gives attorneys a feeling of security that the underlying math doesn't support.

A Google ranking is not a business asset. It is a temporary position in a competitive, algorithmically managed environment. It can be displaced by a competitor with a larger SEO budget, by a Google update that reweights relevance signals, or by the emergence of AI-generated answer boxes that reduce organic click-through rates for informational queries.

That displacement can happen in 90 days. The pipeline gap it creates can take six months to recover.

### What This Means Practically

This isn't an argument to abandon Google. It's an argument to stop treating Google as the load-bearing wall of your lead architecture.

The practices that have navigated algorithm changes, ad cost increases, and market shifts without crisis are the ones that were never dependent on a single source in the first place. They had referral pipelines running in parallel. They had content authority that drove direct searches and direct referrals. They had a follow-up system that reactivated past contacts.

Their intake didn't stop when Google changed the rules. Because Google wasn't their only rule.

## Article 12: The Lead Source Mix That Makes a Family Law Practice Recession-Proof

**Hurdle:** 7 — Google Dependency & Fragile Lead Sources
**ACP Stage Pair:** B2/E2 — Pre-Frame / Contrast
**Angle:** Diversification strategy — what a healthy multi-channel pipeline looks like
**Hook Type:** Contrast — the practices that survived market disruptions vs. those that didn't
**Word Count Target:** 550–700

---

The family law practices that weathered the 2020 slowdown, the 2022 inflation squeeze, and every Google algorithm update in between had one thing in common.

They weren't dependent on a single lead source.

That's not a coincidence. It's the architectural difference between a practice with durable revenue and one that is perpetually one algorithm change or one slow quarter away from a crisis.

### The Three-Source Minimum

A resilient lead architecture for a family law practice typically rests on three distinct source categories operating in parallel. Each one feeds the pipeline in a different way, on a different timeline, and through a different prospect state.

**Referral networks.** Professional referral partners — CPAs, financial advisors, therapists, estate planning attorneys, relationship counselors — see your ideal clients before you do. They see them when the situation is deteriorating but before a decision has been made. A well-developed referral network generates prospects who arrive pre-warmed, pre-qualified, and carrying a trusted endorsement. This is the highest-conversion lead source in family law. It is also the most neglected.

**Content authority and organic search.** Long-form, specific, authoritative content targeting the exact concerns of your ideal client builds compounding visibility. Unlike paid placement, this investment accumulates over time. A well-positioned article about contested business valuation in divorce, published two years ago, may be generating qualified inquiries today without any additional investment. Content authority also builds direct searches — prospects who find your name in an article and search for you by name, bypassing the comparison market entirely.

**Direct response and paid media.** Google Ads, Meta Ads, and LinkedIn Ads are legitimate lead generation channels when used correctly — meaning after the intake process is conversion-ready, and as an amplification layer over an existing foundation, not as a substitute for one. Used this way, paid media can accelerate an already-working system. Used before the foundation exists, it generates leads that don't convert and data that misleads.

### The Sequencing Problem

Most attorneys who over-rely on Google got there not by design but by default. Google Ads worked. They kept investing. Over time, it became the primary — and eventually the only — channel being actively managed.

The referral network got neglected because it takes longer to build and doesn't show up in a dashboard. The content library never grew because it required consistent investment without immediate ROI. The practice optimized for the channel with the fastest feedback loop.

And then Google changed something. Or ad costs rose past the point of profitability. Or a competitor with a larger budget took the top positions.

The practices with a diversified lead architecture don't experience that crisis because their pipeline was never concentrated enough in one place to create it.

### Building the Second and Third Source

If your practice is currently dependent primarily on Google, the goal isn't to abandon it. It's to build two additional lead sources to a point where any single source could disappear without threatening your intake volume.

Start with referrals — specifically, identify the top three professional categories that regularly interact with your ideal client before they file. Develop a systematic way to add value to those relationships quarterly. Six months of consistent investment there will produce a measurable second source.

Build content authority in parallel. One substantive, specific article per month aimed at the exact concerns of your ideal client. It compounds. It doesn't stop working when Google changes.

Three sources running simultaneously makes your practice recession-proof in the only way that matters: your pipeline doesn't stop when any one thing breaks.

## Article 13: Why Paying for Google Ads Before Fixing Your Intake Is Burning Cash

**Hurdle:** 7 — Google Dependency & Fragile Lead Sources
**ACP Stage Pair:** B1/E1 — Enemy Belief / Dissatisfaction
**Angle:** Order of operations — amplifying a broken conversion system
**Hook Type:** Uncomfortable math — most attorneys have done this exact calculation wrong
**Word Count Target:** 450–550

---

May is Better Sleep Month. Here's one thing that will help you sleep better than any mattress upgrade: knowing that the money you're spending on marketing is actually producing clients, not just clicks.

Let's do some math that most attorneys don't want to run.

If your practice runs 20 consultations per month and converts 25% of them, you sign 5 clients. You're paying Google — let's say $3,000 per month — to generate those 20 consultations. That's $600 per client acquired.

Now let's say your intake conversion rate is actually the problem. A structured consultation process, a follow-up sequence, and a retainer framing that connects fees to case-specific risk raises your conversion rate to 40%. Now those same 20 consultations produce 8 clients.

You didn't spend more on ads. You didn't change your rates. You fixed the intake.

The 8-client scenario produces $14,400 more in monthly retainer revenue (at $6,000 average) than the 5-client scenario. Your ad spend didn't change. Your cost per client dropped from $600 to $375.

### The Amplification Problem

Google Ads amplifies whatever is downstream of them.

If your intake process converts at 25%, Google Ads makes you very efficient at generating consultations that mostly don't convert. You are investing marketing dollars to fill a leaking bucket.

The agency running your Google Ads will report your cost per consultation. They will not report your cost per signed client — because that data lives downstream of what they manage, and it is not in their interest to highlight that most of the consultations they generate don't convert.

This is not a knock on Google Ads. It is a sequencing argument. The intake process comes first. The advertising amplification comes after.

### The Right Order

Fix the intake. Measure the conversion rate. Establish a baseline. Then invest in driving more volume through a system that you've confirmed actually works.

The practices that get the best ROI from Google Ads are the ones that built a conversion-ready intake process before they turned the ads on. They know their conversion rate. They know their cost per client. They know what a client is worth over a 12-month engagement. The math on the ads makes sense because the downstream system makes sense.

Run the ads before the intake works and you're paying to learn that your intake doesn't work. That's an expensive lesson with a one-move fix: do the intake work first.

## Article 14: How to Build a Lead System That Doesn't Depend on a Single Platform

**Hurdle:** 7 — Google Dependency & Fragile Lead Sources
**ACP Stage Pair:** B3/E3 — New Worldview / Desire
**Angle:** System architecture — the three-source framework for durable lead generation
**Hook Type:** New frame — a lead system is infrastructure, not a campaign
**Word Count Target:** 550–700

---

May is Moving Month — the time of year when more households relocate than any other. And for family law attorneys, that's not just a demographic fact about your potential clients. It's an apt metaphor: the practices positioned for growth are the ones willing to make a move — to rebuild their lead architecture before the next disruption forces them to.

A lead system built on one platform is not a system. It's a bet.

Most family law attorneys have a collection of tactics — a Google Ads account, a website, maybe a Yelp or Avvo profile — that they refer to collectively as their "marketing." What they don't have is a lead system: a deliberate, multi-channel architecture with clear inputs, defined conversion paths, and enough redundancy that any single component can fail without threatening the practice's growth.

Building that system requires understanding three distinct layers.

### Layer 1 — Owned Infrastructure

Owned infrastructure is the foundation of a durable lead system. It includes your website, your content library, your email list, and your reputation. You control all of these. No platform can take them away.

This layer should be the primary long-term investment for any practice that wants lead generation independence. A strong content library builds organic authority. A structured email list enables direct communication with prospects who have raised their hand. A clear reputation position drives direct searches and direct referrals.

Build this first. Build it consistently. It doesn't produce dramatic short-term results, but it produces results that compound and that no algorithm update can reverse.

### Layer 2 — Relationship-Driven Channels

This layer includes your referral network — the professional relationships that generate introductions to prospects who are already in motion. CPAs, financial advisors, therapists, estate attorneys, and relationship counselors who work with your ideal client profile before, during, and after the kind of life events that lead to family law representation.

This channel requires investment in relationships, not dollars. It builds slowly. But the leads it generates are the highest-converting in the system — because they arrive pre-qualified and pre-endorsed by someone the prospect already trusts.

A practice with a strong referral layer can absorb a Google outage without losing a single client. The phone keeps ringing because the referrals don't stop.

### Layer 3 — Paid Amplification

Paid media — Google, Meta, LinkedIn — belongs in Layer 3, not Layer 1. It amplifies the infrastructure and relationships you've already built. It brings in additional volume when you have the capacity and conversion rate to justify the spend.

Used in this position, paid media is highly effective and highly measurable. Used as the only layer, it is fragile, expensive, and dependent on a platform that doesn't owe you anything.

### Making the Move

If your current lead architecture is primarily or exclusively Layer 3, the work ahead of you is not complicated, but it does require a reordering of investment priorities. Build the owned infrastructure. Develop the referral relationships. Let paid media do what it does best — amplify a system that's already working.

The family law practices that grow without stress, that maintain consistent intake regardless of platform changes, and that build genuine market authority are the ones that made this move early. The ones that kept betting on Layer 3 alone are still waking up to check their Google rankings.

## Article 15: The Referral Network That Generates Leads Without Paying Google

**Hurdle:** 7 — Google Dependency & Fragile Lead Sources
**ACP Stage Pair:** B4/E4 — Internal Alignment / Urgency
**Angle:** Strategic referral network as the best free lead channel — and the most neglected one
**Hook Type:** Specific and actionable — this is closer than most attorneys realize
**Word Count Target:** 500–600

---

May is Foster Care Awareness Month — a month dedicated to building networks of support for children and families at their most vulnerable. The attorneys who most effectively serve families in crisis are often the ones best connected to the professionals who see those families first.

Your best lead source isn't Google. It's not Avvo, Yelp, Martindale, or any other directory.

It's the divorce financial analyst who sees your ideal client six months before they file.

Or the therapist who works with high-conflict couples before the separation decision is made. Or the CPA who handles the business owner's books and watches the marriage deteriorate through the financial statements. Or the estate planning attorney who drafted the prenup three years ago and is now getting calls about whether it holds.

These professionals encounter your ideal client before the client has made any attorney decision. They're trusted advisors in that client's life. And a referral from them carries a weight that no Google Ad can replicate.

### Why This Channel Gets Neglected

Most attorneys know referral networks matter. Few build them with any structure.

What typically happens: an attorney meets a CPA at a networking event, has a good conversation, exchanges cards, and sends a follow-up email. Then both get busy. Six months later, the CPA doesn't remember the attorney specifically — just that they met a family law attorney somewhere.

That's not a relationship. It's an introduction that went nowhere because there was no system to develop it.

The attorneys who consistently generate high-quality referral volume don't have better networks. They have better follow-through. They have a simple, repeatable system for adding value to referral relationships quarterly — a relevant article, a case study, an insight about a topic the referral partner cares about, an introduction that benefits the partner's practice. Not a pitch. Not a newsletter blast. Something specific and useful.

### Where to Start

Identify the three professional categories that most commonly interact with your ideal client type before or during the event that leads to family law representation. For a practice focused on HNW divorce, that list typically includes: CPAs and business accountants, wealth managers and financial advisors, therapists and marriage counselors.

Map those relationships. Who do you already know in each category? Who should you know? For each existing relationship, when did you last provide something genuinely useful?

A deliberate referral development system doesn't require a large network. Five to ten strong referral partners in the right professional categories, maintained with quarterly substance, will outperform thirty passive connections who barely remember your name.

Build those five relationships with intention. The intake volume they generate — and the quality of the prospects they send — will justify every hour you invest.

## Article 16: What Your Google Analytics Dashboard Is Hiding About Your Best Clients

**Hurdle:** 7 — Google Dependency & Fragile Lead Sources
**ACP Stage Pair:** B5/E5 — Certainty / Relief
**Angle:** Attribution gap — the analytics report lies in a specific, important way
**Hook Type:** Revelation — the data you're making decisions based on is incomplete
**Word Count Target:** 450–550

---

Your Google Analytics report says organic search is your top lead source.

It might be wrong.

Not because the data is incorrect — the data is accurate. It's measuring what it can measure. The problem is what it can't measure, and what it's defaulting to when the real source is invisible to it.

### The Last-Click Problem

Most web analytics platforms, including Google Analytics in its default configuration, attribute a visit to whatever channel generated the last click before the prospect arrived on your site.

Here's what that means in practice.

A financial advisor tells a client: "You need to talk to [attorney name]. I've sent a few people to her and they all say she's excellent." The client goes home, opens a browser, and types the attorney's name directly into Google. Google Analytics records this as a "direct" visit or an "organic search" visit.

The referral — the actual origin of that prospect's interest — is invisible in the report.

This happens constantly. The referral network generates the intent. The digital channel gets the credit. And the attorney, looking at the dashboard, concludes that their top lead source is organic search or direct traffic, and invests accordingly.

### Why This Matters for Your Decisions

If your analytics are systematically attributing referral-originated traffic to digital channels, you're making investment decisions based on incomplete information.

You may be increasing your Google Ads budget because the dashboard shows traffic volume increasing, while the actual driver of your best clients — the referral network — is getting no investment because it doesn't show up in any report.

### The Fix

The simplest fix is intake attribution: ask every new consultation how they heard about you. Not just what your form says — actually ask. "Before we dive in, can you tell me specifically how you found us? Did someone recommend us?"

The answer will frequently differ from what your analytics report. Track both. Over six months, the pattern will become clear: which channel is actually driving your best clients, and which channel is getting the credit.

Make investment decisions based on actual attribution, not platform-reported attribution. The practices that have done this work often discover that their highest-value clients came from referrals they weren't systematically developing — and shift resources accordingly.

What you can't measure, you can't manage. Measure the right thing.

## Article 17: Why the Agency That Built Your Website Has No Idea If It's Working

**Hurdle:** 8 — The Marketing Agency Betrayal
**ACP Stage Pair:** B1/E1 — Enemy Belief / Dissatisfaction
**Angle:** Accountability gap — agencies measure the wrong metrics and call it success
**Hook Type:** Uncomfortable truth — the report they send you is measuring the wrong thing
**Word Count Target:** 450–550

---

Every month, the agency that manages your website sends you a report.

It probably shows traffic numbers. Impressions. Click-through rate. Average session duration. Maybe some keyword ranking movement. The numbers might be trending in the right direction. It looks like progress.

Here's what the report almost certainly does not show: how many of that traffic converted to consultations. How many consultations converted to signed clients. What the cost per acquired client was. Whether any of those clients were the right kind of client for your practice.

That gap — between what the agency reports and what actually matters to your practice — is not an accident. It reflects the fundamental misalignment between what agencies are designed to deliver and what family law practices actually need.

### What Agencies Are Optimized For

Digital marketing agencies are optimized to demonstrate activity. They drive traffic, improve rankings, generate clicks. These are the metrics they can control, and they report on them enthusiastically.

What they cannot control — and therefore do not optimize for — is what happens after the click. Your consultation structure. Your intake process. Your retainer conversion rate. Your client fit criteria. These are downstream of the agency's deliverables and outside their scope.

The result is a practice that may have excellent traffic metrics and a struggling intake pipeline — and no diagnostic framework that connects the two.

### The Missing Metric

The single most important metric in a family law marketing stack is cost per signed client.

Not cost per click. Not cost per consultation. Cost per signed client — the actual revenue-generating outcome of all marketing investment.

Most agencies don't track this because they don't have visibility into your intake data. Most attorneys don't track it because they've never been asked to.

When you start tracking cost per signed client, two things happen: you immediately understand which marketing channels are actually working, and you identify where the conversion bottleneck is. Often the bottleneck isn't in the marketing at all. It's in the intake process downstream of it.

The agency isn't going to tell you this. They don't have the data. And even if they did, identifying a downstream conversion problem is not something most agencies are equipped to solve.

You need to track it yourself. And you need to find partners who understand what the number means.

## Article 18: What a Legal Marketing Agency Sells You vs. What You Actually Need

**Hurdle:** 8 — The Marketing Agency Betrayal
**ACP Stage Pair:** B2/E2 — Pre-Frame / Contrast
**Angle:** Mismatch between agency deliverables and practice growth
**Hook Type:** Side-by-side comparison — what's being sold vs. what moves the needle
**Word Count Target:** 550–650

---

There's a consistent pattern in how family law attorneys describe their experience with marketing agencies. The pitch was compelling. The first few months showed activity. Then the results plateaued. Then the contract renewed. Then you started having the same conversation you had before you hired the agency — where are the clients?

That pattern isn't a coincidence. It reflects a structural mismatch between what marketing agencies are built to sell and what family law practices actually need to grow.

### What the Agency Sells

A typical legal marketing agency will offer some combination of:

- Website design and development
- Search engine optimization (organic rankings)
- Google Ads management (paid placement)
- Social media management
- Review generation
- Monthly reporting on traffic, rankings, and impressions

These are real services with real value. They're also upstream of the thing that actually generates revenue: a qualified prospect who speaks with your intake team and signs a retainer.

### What You Actually Need

What a family law practice at the managing partner level needs to grow is a complete system — from initial awareness through signed client — that is optimized at every stage for the right kind of client.

That means positioning that attracts the right prospect before they ever visit the website. A website that converts the visitor into a consultation request, not just a page view. An intake process that converts the consultation into a signed retainer at a consistent rate. A follow-up system that recaptures the prospects who didn't sign on the first contact. A referral system that generates introductions from professional partners who see your ideal client first.

Most agencies deliver pieces of this. Almost none deliver the complete system. And the pieces they do deliver — traffic, rankings, clicks — are easy to produce and easy to report on, which makes them the natural default in an agency-client relationship that's measured primarily by activity metrics.

### Why the Gap Persists

Agencies are not incentivized to tell you that your traffic is fine and your intake is broken. Solving the intake problem is outside their scope, and pointing to it would redirect your attention away from the services they provide.

So the conversation stays at the traffic layer. Rankings improve. Sessions increase. And the managing partner wonders why the phone isn't ringing with the right clients.

The gap persists because the person responsible for diagnosing it — the agency — has a structural incentive not to find it.

Closing that gap requires a different kind of partner: one who is responsible for the full pipeline, not just the top of it, and who is measured by client acquisition outcomes, not activity reports.

## Article 19: What "We Specialize in Law Firm Marketing" Actually Means (And What It Doesn't)

**Hurdle:** 8 — The Marketing Agency Betrayal
**ACP Stage Pair:** B3/E3 — New Worldview / Desire
**Angle:** Decoding the specialization claim — what it usually means vs. what you need
**Hook Type:** Translation — what the agency means and what the attorney hears are two different things
**Word Count Target:** 500–600

---

May is International Business Image Improvement Month — a month that prompts businesses to examine how they're presenting themselves to the market. Here's an image problem worth examining: the marketing agency that claims to specialize in law firm marketing, when what they've actually built is a template-driven production system for a category as broad as law.

When an agency says "we specialize in law firm marketing," here's what that usually means.

They've worked with personal injury firms at scale. They have a proven paid search playbook for personal injury keywords that has a consistent cost-per-lead benchmark. They've possibly worked with estate planning practices, business law firms, or DUI defense attorneys.

What it usually does not mean: they understand the specific economics of a high-net-worth family law practice. The long case lifecycle. The retainer-based intake model. The referral network architecture that drives the best clients. The difference between a consultation that converts and one that doesn't. The positioning work that makes an attorney the obvious choice for a specific client profile rather than a general option in a comparison market.

### The Template Problem

Agencies that serve multiple law firm practice areas have templates. They have a website template that works for law firms. They have a Google Ads framework that works for legal services. They have a content calendar that works for professional service businesses.

These templates produce acceptable results. They rarely produce exceptional ones, because exceptional results in family law marketing come from specificity — from positioning, messaging, and intake systems that are built for the exact client type you serve and the exact problems you solve.

A family law specialist serving HNW clients is not running a personal injury firm with higher retainers. The buyer psychology is different. The trust threshold is different. The referral architecture is different. The content strategy is different. The intake conversion process is different.

An agency with a law firm template doesn't know most of this. They know what works for law firms generally — which in a niche market is only a fraction of what needs to work.

### What to Ask Instead

When evaluating a marketing partner for a family law practice at your level, the relevant questions are not about general legal marketing experience. They're specific:

Have you worked specifically with HNW family law practices? What was the client's average retainer when you started and when you finished? What was their consultation-to-signed-client conversion rate? How did you develop and measure the referral network component? What was the intake framework you used?

If the answers are vague or pivot back to traffic metrics and keyword rankings, you're looking at a law firm template agency. That may be fine for some practices. For a high-value practice built on specific client quality, it's a structural mismatch.

## Article 20: The Five Questions to Ask Before Hiring Any Marketing Partner for Your Law Firm

**Hurdle:** 8 — The Marketing Agency Betrayal
**ACP Stage Pair:** B3/E3 — New Worldview / Desire
**Angle:** Buyer intelligence — how to identify a genuine partner vs. a capable vendor
**Hook Type:** Practical checklist — most attorneys hire based on pitch quality, not the right questions
**Word Count Target:** 500–650

---

Most family law attorneys hire marketing partners the same way they hire vendors: based on presentation quality, price, and gut instinct after a sales call.

That approach produces the hiring-and-firing cycle that many managing partners know well. The agency pitches well. The first few months feel like progress. Then the results stall. Then you're evaluating another agency.

The cycle ends when you start asking the right questions before you sign.

Here are five.

### Question 1: "What is your definition of a successful engagement, and how do you measure it?"

The answer reveals what the agency is optimizing for. If they describe success in terms of traffic, rankings, or impressions, they're measuring activity, not outcomes. The right answer centers on client acquisition — signed retainers — and describes a clear mechanism for tracking from first contact to signed agreement.

### Question 2: "Have you worked with high-net-worth family law practices specifically, and what were the measurable outcomes?"

Not "law firms generally." Not "family law attorneys in your region." Specifically high-net-worth family law practices — where the intake model, referral architecture, and client psychology are distinct from other legal service categories. If the case studies they present are personal injury scale campaigns or general family law practices at a moderate retainer level, that's a signal about their actual experience with your problem.

### Question 3: "How do you approach the intake conversion piece — what happens after someone fills out your contact form?"

This question identifies whether the agency's scope ends at the click or extends into the conversion process. Most agencies' scope ends at the click. The intake conversion piece — consultation structure, retainer framing, follow-up sequencing — is where most qualified leads are lost, and most agencies have no framework for it.

A partner who can answer this question specifically, with a methodology, is operating at a different level than one who pivots back to their traffic strategy.

### Question 4: "What does your referral network development process look like for a practice like mine?"

Referrals are the highest-converting lead source in family law. If the agency doesn't have a referral network development process — and most don't, because it doesn't fit into a digital agency's service model — you have a clear picture of a critical gap in their system.

### Question 5: "How are you compensated, and how does that align with our mutual interest in signed clients?"

An agency paid on retainer for activity is not financially aligned with your growth. An agency compensated on outcomes — signed clients, revenue generated — has skin in the same game you do. The compensation structure reveals where the incentives actually sit, which is a more reliable predictor of behavior than anything in the pitch deck.

These five questions won't find you a perfect partner on their own. But they will efficiently separate the agencies who understand your specific problem from the ones who understand agencies' specific problem — which is selling engagements, not building practices.
