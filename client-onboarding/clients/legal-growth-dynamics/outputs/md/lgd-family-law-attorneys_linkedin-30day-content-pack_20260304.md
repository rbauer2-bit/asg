### The Avatar: The Capped Solo or Small Firm Family Law Attorney

They run a practice of 1 to 6 attorneys. Revenue is somewhere between $350K and $1.8M and has been there for longer than they'd like to admit. They are genuinely skilled. Their clients get real outcomes. But their pipeline is almost entirely referral-dependent — which means it is entirely out of their control.

They have tried marketing before. Maybe they paid a local agency for a website redesign and some SEO work. Maybe they ran Google Ads for three months and got tire-kickers. Whatever it was, it didn't work the way they hoped. That experience calcified into a belief they now carry as fact: *marketing doesn't work for family law attorneys.*

On LinkedIn, they scroll past marketing content from business coaches and agency owners without engaging because it doesn't feel like it applies to them. They are waiting for someone who actually understands their world — the billable hour, the bar rules, the emotional weight of the cases, the impossibility of "niching down" when someone calls about their children.

Roger is that person. This content pack is designed to prove it — post by post, over 30 days.

**Primary Enemy Belief (B1):** "Family law clients don't come from content or LinkedIn — they come from referrals. If I put time into content marketing, I'll waste it and look unprofessional doing it."

### The Emotional Journey Across 30 Days

**Week 1 (Days 1–6): Named and Seen**
The attorney reads these posts and has the experience of being *described accurately* — their frustrations, their beliefs, their specific market situation. This is the rarest thing in any marketing they've encountered. They don't feel sold to. They feel understood.

**Week 2 (Days 7–12): The Lens Shifts**
The pre-frame posts challenge assumptions without attacking them. The attorney starts to see their situation differently — not because they've been convinced of something new, but because the existing evidence starts pointing somewhere they hadn't looked.

**Week 3 (Days 13–19): They Want It**
New Worldview posts and Desire triggers show them what a systematically grown family law practice actually looks like. The authority engine concept is introduced as a description, not a pitch. They start to want the outcome — predictable pipeline, better clients, cases they actually want to take.

**Week 4 (Days 20–25): The Cost of Waiting Becomes Real**
Internal Alignment posts explain why past attempts didn't work — removing self-blame and redirecting it toward a systems problem they can solve. Urgency posts surface the genuine cost of another year without a pipeline. The survey is introduced.

**Week 5–6 (Days 26–30): Action Is Safe**
The attorney reaches the survey with two things simultaneously in place: intellectual certainty that a real solution exists for their practice, and emotional relief that the first step is a five-minute assessment with no commitment attached.

**Stage:** B1/E1 — Enemy Belief + Dissatisfaction
**Pillar:** Insider View

---

**DOPAMINE LADDER BREAKDOWN**

**L1 — STIMULATION (Hooks)**
- **Main:** I've looked at 200+ family law websites across the country. Almost every one has the same problem.
- **Alt 1:** If I audited your law firm's website right now, here's what I'd find in under 60 seconds.
- **Alt 2:** Family law firms spend thousands on websites that actively cost them high-value cases. Most don't know it.

**L2 — CAPTIVATION**
The problem isn't the design. It isn't even the copy. It's something structural — and it's costing these firms the cases they actually want.

**L3 — ANTICIPATION**
These websites describe. They list credentials. They show a headshot, a bar admission year, a list of practice areas. Some have blog posts from 2021. A few have videos that autoplay with the attorney staring at the camera looking vaguely uncomfortable.

And none of that is the problem. The problem is that none of it answers the one question every high-stakes family law client is asking when they land on your site.

**L4 — VALIDATION**
The question isn't "Is this attorney qualified?" They assume you're qualified — you passed the bar. The question is: "Do you understand cases like mine? Have you seen this before? Do you know what I'm up against?"

Credentials don't answer that question. Case-specific authority does.

**L5 — AFFECTION**
The attorneys I've worked with who made this shift — even a small one — didn't just get more traffic. They started getting calls from people who already wanted to hire them before they ever spoke to anyone.

**L6 — REVELATION**
The best family law website isn't the most beautiful one. It's the one that makes the right client feel found.

The goal isn't to replace referrals. It's to stop being one phone call away from a slow quarter.
