Today is World Autism Awareness Day — a day dedicated to making the invisible visible, to recognizing what's happening beneath the surface that most of us walk right past without seeing.

[B-ROLL: gentle visual of someone looking in the mirror, studying their face/mouth — relatable, non-clinical]

I'm Dr. Jeff Hedges in Louisville, Kentucky. April is National Oral Health Month, and if there's one piece of information I wish every person in this city had, it's what I'm about to tell you.

[B-ROLL: dental X-ray showing a missing tooth space — arrow pointing to the gap and surrounding bone level]

So here's what happens when a tooth is missing and isn't replaced. The jawbone underneath that empty space — the alveolar bone — no longer receives the stimulation it needs to maintain its density. Teeth roots stimulate bone during chewing. Without that stimulation, the bone begins to resorb. It shrinks. Not immediately — but consistently, over months and years.

[B-ROLL: medical animation or diagram showing bone resorption over time — 6 months, 1 year, 3 years comparison]

This matters for implants because an implant needs adequate bone volume and density to anchor into. The longer a patient waits after tooth loss, the more complex — and often more expensive — the implant placement becomes. Bone grafting may be required. Healing time increases. The case that could have been straightforward 18 months ago is now a staged procedure with additional cost.

That's not a scare tactic. That's just biology.

[B-ROLL: Dr. Hedges looking at a 3D CBCT scan, pointing to bone measurements on screen]

Let me give you the five signs I see in patients who have been waiting too long.

**Sign One: The teeth next to the gap are tilting.**

When a tooth is missing, the neighboring teeth lose their structural reference point. Over time — sometimes within months — they begin to drift into the space. You'll see this on an X-ray as a visible tilt. Sometimes patients feel it as a change in their bite. Either way, it complicates the implant placement because the space has narrowed.

[B-ROLL: X-ray showing tooth drift toward empty space — visible tilt]

**Sign Two: The opposing tooth is super-erupting.**

The tooth directly above or below the missing space has no tooth to bite against. Without that contact, it begins to grow downward — or upward — beyond its normal position. This is called super-eruption. I've seen cases where a patient waited three years and the opposing tooth had erupted far enough that it would have to be addressed before an implant could even be considered.

[B-ROLL: diagram or model showing super-eruption concept]

**Sign Three: You've noticed a change in your bite.**

If you've started chewing differently — favoring one side, noticing that your back teeth hit differently than they used to — this is your jaw compensating for missing structure. Over time this compensation creates strain on the remaining teeth, on the jaw joint, and on the muscles. Patients come to me with headaches and jaw pain who don't realize the root cause is a missing tooth they've been ignoring for two years.

[B-ROLL: patient jaw joint area, touching the side of their face with a slightly uncomfortable expression]

**Sign Four: You can see a visible indentation in your gumline.**

Stand in front of a mirror and look where the missing tooth was. If you can see a noticeable depression — a concave shape in the gumline — that's a visual indicator of bone loss already in progress. Some patients assume that's normal. It isn't. It's your jaw changing shape.

[B-ROLL: close-up of gumline showing depression where tooth is missing — not graphic, clinical illustration preferred]

They're watching a situation change from simple to complex in slow motion and trying to give you the opportunity to catch it before it does.

**Innovation One: Cone Beam CT — 3D Surgical Planning**

I've referenced CBCT scans throughout this month. Today I want to explain specifically what they changed.

Prior to CBCT becoming standard, implant placement was planned from 2D panoramic X-rays. A panoramic shows height — how tall the available bone is. It does not show width — how thick the bone is front to back. It cannot show the precise location of the inferior alveolar nerve, which runs through the lower jaw and must be avoided during implant placement. It cannot show the exact shape of the sinus floor in the upper jaw.

A CBCT scan produces a full three-dimensional model of your jaw. I can measure bone height, bone width, bone density, and the precise anatomical landmarks — nerve canal, sinus floor, adjacent tooth roots — that define the safe placement zone before I've picked up an instrument. I can plan the angle, depth, and diameter of the implant with millimeter precision.

The result: more predictable placement, fewer complications, and in many cases the ability to place implants in sites that would have been declined as "too risky" under 2D planning.

[B-ROLL: CBCT 3D image on screen — rotating, showing bone volume from multiple angles — genuinely impressive clinical tool]

**Innovation Two: Guided Surgery**

Once a CBCT scan is complete, the plan can be translated into a surgical guide — a custom-fabricated template that fits precisely over the teeth or gums and directs the implant drill to the exact planned position, angle, and depth.

Guided surgery removes the largest variable in implant placement: the moment-to-moment clinical judgment of freehand drilling. The guide eliminates angular deviation. It constrains depth. It makes the execution of the plan match the plan itself.

[B-ROLL: surgical guide being placed — clean, precise, clinical but not intimidating]

For patients, what this means: shorter surgery time in many cases, more predictable healing, and in some cases — particularly where anatomy is complex — outcomes that would not have been achievable with freehand technique.

The final restoration that sits on your implants has also changed significantly. The older standard for full-arch prostheses was acrylic — a composite material that looked good initially but was susceptible to chipping, staining, and wear over time, typically requiring replacement at 7–10 years.
