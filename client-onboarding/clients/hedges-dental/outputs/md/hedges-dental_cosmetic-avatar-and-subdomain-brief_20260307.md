# PART 1: LOUISVILLE SMILE EMILY — AVATAR DEFINITION

*Architecture: Dr. Raymond Cross, Board | Application: Vivienne Carr, CMO*

## AVATAR PROFILE

**Name:** Louisville Smile Emily
**Age:** 32–48
**Gender:** Female
**Location:** East Louisville suburbs — St. Matthews, Lyndon, Brownsboro, Prospect, Crestwood, or Middletown
**Household income:** $85,000–$175,000 (HHI, dual income or professional single)
**Occupation:** Professional, healthcare worker, educator, business owner, executive-adjacent, or stay-at-home parent with professional background
**Marital status:** Married or partnered (primary), recently divorced or entering new relationship (secondary)
**Children:** Typically 1–3, school age or older

---

## PSYCHOLOGICAL PROFILE

Emily's dental concern is not clinical. She doesn't have missing teeth. She's not in pain. She brushes, she goes to her cleanings, she is by most definitions a good dental patient.

What Emily has is a specific, persistent awareness of one thing about her smile that she wishes were different. It has a name:

- Her teeth are yellowed — coffee, wine, years of mild neglect she wishes she could reverse
- Her front teeth are chipped or worn — not dramatically, but noticeably to her
- Her smile is uneven in a way that shows in photos
- She was told years ago that veneers would "fix" the specific thing she dislikes but she's never acted

This isn't a new concern. Emily has thought about her smile for years. What changes in the window when she becomes a patient is a specific event or life context that moves the concern from background noise to actionable priority.

---

## TRIGGER EVENTS — WHAT ACTIVATES EMILY

Emily doesn't wake up one day and decide to address her smile. Something happens that makes the concern feel urgent in a way it didn't before.

The most common triggers:

**Event-based:**
- An upcoming wedding (her own, a daughter's, a sister's) — with the understanding that there will be photos
- A high school or college reunion
- A significant milestone birthday (40th, 45th, 50th)
- A professional event (promotion, new position, speaking engagement, headshots)
- A new relationship — or re-entering the dating world after a divorce

**Identity-based:**
- A photo that crystallized the concern ("I saw my teeth in that picture and I thought, I need to do something")
- A friend or colleague who had cosmetic work done and whose transformation Emily noticed
- A general season of self-investment — gym, wardrobe, skincare — where the smile becomes the next category

**Life-context-based:**
- Kids are older now, more disposable income
- A period of deferred self-care ending ("I've been last for ten years and I'm finally doing things for me")
- Discretionary spending that has opened up after a major expense (house renovated, kids' tuitions decreasing, etc.)

---

## ENEMY BELIEF

> *"Cosmetic dentistry is vain. It's expensive, it's elective, and prioritizing it means I'm being superficial. I should wait until I actually need something — or until [milestone event] passes and I have more time."*

**Why this belief forms:**
Emily is a practical, competent adult. She has spent years prioritizing other people — her children, her partner, her career obligations. Spending $8,000–$18,000 on something that is, in the technical dental sense, elective, triggers an internal guilt mechanism that shows up as deferral.

She doesn't say "this is too expensive." She says "I'm not sure this is the right time."
She doesn't say "I'm afraid." She says "I just haven't gotten around to it."

The enemy belief is the story she tells herself to justify deferral. It is not a financial objection. It is an identity objection — the belief that wanting this makes her less serious, less responsible, or less entitled to it.

**What produces the transition:**
Permission-granting content that reframes cosmetic dentistry as an act of investment in self-presentation — which is professional, social, and psychological, not vain. The frame that resonates: "Your smile affects how you show up. That matters." Not aspirational. Direct.

## FULL BELIEF-TO-BUY FRAMEWORK MAP

### BELIEF TRACK

**B1 — ENEMY BELIEF**
*Avatar's internal monologue:*
> "I've thought about this for years. But it feels like something people do when they have extra money and no real priorities. I have kids. A mortgage. Real things to spend money on. This can wait."

*What produces the transition:*
Content that validates the practical mindset AND reframes what's actually at stake. Not lifestyle aspiration — professional and personal self-presentation. The frame: "You already invest in your professional appearance. Why is your smile the exception?"

*Content piece:*
"Why the patients who get veneers always say they wish they'd done it sooner — a Louisville dentist's honest take." (Vivienne Carr / Sienna Okafor — LinkedIn + blog)

---

**B2 — PRE-FRAME**
*Avatar's internal monologue:*
> "Okay, maybe I've been telling myself this is vain when really it's just... practical self-investment. But I still don't fully understand what the process actually looks like. Or what veneers even are, specifically."

*What produces the transition:*
Process transparency — demystifying what veneers, whitening, and cosmetic consultations actually involve. Emily is a researcher. She wants to understand the procedure before she decides. Educational content that walks through the process without jargon earns her trust faster than any aspirational imagery.

*Content piece:*
"What happens at a cosmetic dental consultation — and what Louisville patients are surprised to learn." (Blog article / YouTube video — Jade Calloway)

---

**B3 — NEW WORLDVIEW**
*Avatar's internal monologue:*
> "The process sounds more manageable than I expected. And the results look genuinely natural — not that obvious fake-teeth look I was afraid of. The real question is whether the timing and cost make sense for my situation."

*What produces the transition:*
Before/after galleries showing natural, age-appropriate results — not cosmetically maximalist, not celebrities. Louisville women in their 30s–40s. Results that look like a better version of themselves, not a different person. Combined with transparent cost framing.

*Content piece:*
Before/after gallery on cosmetic.hedgesdental.com with realistic cost framing. "What Louisville cosmetic dental patients are paying — and what they got."

---

**B4 — INTERNAL ALIGNMENT**
*Avatar's internal monologue:*
> "She looks exactly like herself, just... better. And she said she wasn't scared. The consultation was the part that convinced her. I want that consultation."

*What produces the transition:*
A patient story that matches Emily's identity — same approximate age, similar life context, similar hesitation. The story is not about the procedure. It's about what changed after. The reunion she attended. The headshots she finally felt good about. The photos she stopped avoiding.

*Content piece:*
Patient story: "[Name], Louisville — 'I scheduled it two weeks before my 40th birthday. I wish I'd done it at 35.'"

---

**B5 — CERTAINTY**
*Avatar's internal monologue:*
> "I know what I want. I know what it costs. I know Dr. Hedges does this at 2015 Herr Lane and that it's the same team as the implant side of his practice. I just need to book."

*What produces the transition:*
A clear, low-friction path to scheduling. The cosmetic consultation page removes every remaining barrier — what to expect, what it costs, what happens next.

*Content piece:*

### EMOTION TRACK

**E1 — DISSATISFACTION**
*Emotional state:*
Emily has normalized her cosmetic concern. It surfaces in photo moments — the slight turn of the head, the closed-mouth smile, the crop in Instagram. It's not acute suffering. It's a quiet, persistent awareness of a gap between how she looks and how she wants to look.

The dissatisfaction becomes active in the trigger events listed above — when the photo moment has a date on the calendar, when the promotion puts her in front of people, when the reunion is six months away.

*What activates this stage:*
Timing-relevant content. "You have a [wedding/reunion/milestone] in the next six months? Here's what cosmetic timeline actually looks like." Content that connects Emily's event to the lead time required for veneers.

---

**E2 — CONTRAST**
*Emotional state:*
Emily has now seen a before/after that looks like her. Not a celebrity's transformation. A Louisville woman, 38 or 43, who had a similar concern and got a result that looks natural and genuinely better.

The emotion here is: "That could be me. That could look like that."

*What produces this:*
High-quality, natural before/after photography on cosmetic.hedgesdental.com. The "after" photos should show women in real contexts — professional headshots, smiling at family events — not just a dental close-up. The emotional contrast is between who Emily is now and who she could be.

---

**E3 — DESIRE**
*Emotional state:*
Emily is actively researching. She has a specific provider on her shortlist. She's comparing consult processes, reading reviews, looking at before/after galleries from multiple practices. She wants a reason to make a decision.

*What produces this:*
Social proof that is identity-specific. Google reviews that specifically describe the cosmetic consultation experience — warm, unhurried, no pressure. Reviews that say "I finally felt like someone was listening to what I actually wanted" are more persuasive to Emily than any clinical credential.

---

**E4 — URGENCY**
*Emotional state:*
Emily's trigger event has a date. Or she's watched herself in the mirror for long enough. The question isn't "if" anymore — it's "before it's too late to be ready for the event" or "before I talk myself out of it again."

*What produces this:*
Timing-transparent content: "Porcelain veneers take 2–3 appointments over 3–4 weeks. Whitening is 2 weeks. If your event is in [X] months, here's what's still possible."

---

**E5 — RELIEF**
*Emotional state:*
Emily is booked. She arrives at the consultation. Dr. Hedges listens. He shows her what her options are. He shows her what results look like. There's no pressure. She makes a decision that feels like her own.

The relief is: "This was so much easier than I made it in my head. I should have done this years ago."

*What produces this:*
A consultation experience that delivers on the promise of every content piece that came before it. No hard close. No financing urgency. A clinical conversation that respects Emily's intelligence and honors her timeline.

## STRATEGIC CASE

**The opportunity:** Emily is searching "porcelain veneers Louisville," "teeth whitening Louisville KY," and "smile makeover Louisville." She is not on TopLouisvilleDentist.com — that site speaks to Rachel, not Emily. She needs her own entry point.

**The domain decision:** cosmetic.hedgesdental.com (subdomain) — not a new root domain. Rationale: Emily's search volume and competitive urgency do not justify starting a new domain from zero authority. The subdomain borrows hedgesdental.com's existing domain signals while targeting a distinct keyword cluster and avatar conversation.

**Duplicate content strategy:**
- hedgesdental.com/services/veneers = B2 informational (what veneers are, the process)
- cosmetic.hedgesdental.com/veneers = B4/B5 conversion (patient stories, natural results gallery, specific cases, consultation CTA)
- Different keyword cluster: broad "veneers Louisville" (main site) vs. "porcelain veneers Louisville smile makeover," "wedding smile Louisville," "veneers vs Invisalign Louisville" (subdomain)
- `rel=canonical` on subdomain pages pointing to main site as backstop only — unique content is the primary protection
