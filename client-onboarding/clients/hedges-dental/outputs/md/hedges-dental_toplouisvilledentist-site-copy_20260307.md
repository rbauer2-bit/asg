If you've been missing most or all of your teeth — or if you're wearing a denture that never quite fits right, never quite stays in, and never quite lets you eat what you want to eat — full-arch dental implants are worth understanding.

The concept is simpler than the name suggests.

Full-arch implants go by several names — All-on-4, All-on-6, All-on-X, Teeth in a Day. The specifics of your treatment depend on your jaw anatomy, bone density, and the number of teeth being replaced. Dr. Hedges will review your cone beam CT scan and discuss which approach fits your situation.

### SECTION: THE HONEST CASE FOR AN IMPLANT

When you lose a single tooth, you have three options.

You can do nothing. The gap closes — not because the teeth grow back, but because adjacent teeth begin to drift toward the space. Bone at the extraction site begins to resorb. Within 12 months, the structural change in your jaw is measurable. Within five years, the shifting is visible. What began as a single missing tooth becomes an orthodontic and restorative problem.

You can get a bridge. A bridge is a fixed prosthetic that covers the gap by anchoring to the two healthy teeth on either side. Those teeth are permanently altered — ground down to support the crowns that anchor the bridge. You're trading two healthy teeth to replace one missing one. The bridge itself typically lasts 10–15 years before replacement.

You can get an implant. A titanium post is placed into your jaw at the site of the missing tooth. Over 3–6 months, the post fuses with your bone — a process called osseointegration. A natural-looking crown is then attached to the post. The result is a tooth replacement that functions like the natural tooth it replaces, preserves the bone at the site, doesn't require altering adjacent teeth, and, in most cases, lasts a lifetime with normal care.

Most implant dentists will tell you the same thing: if you're a candidate for an implant and the choice is between a bridge and an implant, the implant is the better long-term decision. It costs more upfront. Over a 20-year horizon, the numbers usually reverse.

---

### SECTION: WHAT THE PROCESS LOOKS LIKE

**Consultation and imaging (free at Hedges Dental)**
Dr. Hedges evaluates the extraction site, reviews bone density, and determines whether you're a candidate for direct placement or whether bone grafting is needed first. A cone beam CT scan provides the three-dimensional view required to plan placement precisely.

**Bone grafting (if needed)**
If the extraction site has insufficient bone, a bone graft is placed — typically at the time of extraction, if the tooth hasn't already been removed. Healing takes 3–6 months before the implant post can be placed.

**Implant placement**
Under local anesthesia, Dr. Hedges places the titanium post. The site is sutured and allowed to heal. A temporary crown is placed if aesthetics require it during the healing period.

**Healing (osseointegration): 3–6 months**
The implant integrates with your jaw bone. This is the biological process that makes implants permanent — the titanium fuses with live bone.

**Crown delivery**
Once integration is confirmed, your final crown is attached to the implant post. The crown is matched to the color, shape, and size of your adjacent teeth. The result looks and functions like a natural tooth.

### H1: Implant-Retained Dentures in Louisville
#### When a Traditional Denture Isn't Enough

---

### SECTION: IF YOUR DENTURE DOESN'T STAY PUT, THERE'S A REASON

A traditional removable denture rests on your gum tissue and relies on suction and adhesive to stay in place. This works — to a degree — in the years immediately after tooth loss. Over time, it works less well.

The reason is bone resorption. When teeth are lost, the jaw bone that once supported them begins to shrink. This happens continuously. Over years, the shape of your jaw changes — and a denture designed to fit your gums five years ago no longer fits the gums you have today. The result is a denture that shifts when you eat, moves when you speak, and requires more adhesive with every passing year.

Implant-retained dentures solve the underlying problem rather than working around it.

Two to four small implants are placed in the jaw. The denture attaches to these implants using a snap-on mechanism — it can be removed for cleaning, but during the day it's locked in position. It doesn't slide. It doesn't click. It doesn't require adhesive. And because the implants are stimulating the jaw bone where they're placed, bone resorption slows significantly.

---

### SECTION: IS AN IMPLANT-RETAINED DENTURE RIGHT FOR YOU?

You may be a good candidate if you:
- Wear a current removable denture that fits poorly, causes soreness, or moves during eating or speaking
- Are edentulous (missing all teeth in one arch) and looking for a more stable solution
- Want the improved function of implants but prefer a removable prosthetic for cleaning
- Are not ready for or don't qualify for full-arch fixed implants

Unlike full-arch fixed implant treatment (All-on-4), implant-retained dentures typically require fewer implants and involve a lower overall cost — while delivering significantly better stability and function than a conventional denture.

Dr. Hedges will evaluate your bone density, jaw anatomy, and current denture at your consultation and give you a clear recommendation on which option fits your situation.

---

Typically two to four per arch for a lower denture, and four to six for an upper. The upper jaw requires more implants because the bone density and implant surface area needed for suction replacement is higher. Dr. Hedges will specify the number at your consultation based on your imaging.

## ARTICLE 3

**Title:** What Happens to Your Jaw When a Missing Tooth Goes Untreated (And Why Timing Matters)
**Target keywords:** bone loss missing tooth Louisville, tooth replacement Louisville, dental implant timing Louisville
**B/E Stage:** B4/E4 — Urgency (bone loss education)
**URL:** /blog/bone-loss-missing-tooth-louisville

---

The question most patients ask when they're missing a tooth is: *"How does this look?"*

The more clinically important question is: *"What is happening to my jaw right now?"*

The answer to the second question is the reason why implant dentists consistently recommend addressing tooth loss sooner rather than later — not as a sales tactic, but as a clinical reality that directly affects the complexity and cost of eventual treatment.

**What happens to bone after a tooth is lost**

Every tooth in your mouth is embedded in the alveolar bone — the bone of the jaw that exists specifically to support teeth. When you chew, the force transmitted through your tooth into this bone is what signals the bone to maintain itself. Remove the tooth and that signal stops.

Within the first three months after an extraction, bone density at the site begins to decline. By 12 months, the width of the alveolar bone at the extraction site has typically decreased by 25 percent. By three years, the loss can reach 40–60 percent of original width, with corresponding height loss as the process continues.

This isn't visible in a mirror. But it's measurable on a cone beam CT scan — and it directly affects your implant candidacy.

**Why this matters for implant treatment**

Implant posts require sufficient bone volume to achieve osseointegration — the process by which titanium fuses with living bone. A post placed in inadequate bone has a higher failure rate and may not achieve the stability required to support a crown under biting force.

When bone volume is insufficient, bone grafting is required before implant placement. Grafting restores bone volume by encouraging the growth of new bone — using synthetic material, donor material, or bone harvested from another site. Grafting adds cost (typically $500–$1,500 per site) and adds 3–6 months to the treatment timeline before implant placement can proceed.

**The clinical math**

A patient who presents for a single-tooth implant within six months of extraction — when bone volume is still adequate — typically completes treatment in 4–6 months without grafting.

The same patient who waits three years may require grafting, extending the timeline to 8–12 months and adding $1,000–$1,500 to the cost.

Neither outcome is a catastrophe. The implant can be placed successfully in both cases. But the difference in cost, complexity, and timeline is real — and it's entirely determined by when the decision gets made.

**Adjacent teeth**

Bone loss isn't the only thing happening when a tooth is missing. The teeth on either side of the gap experience the change in structural support. Without the missing tooth's presence, adjacent teeth begin to drift — slowly tilting toward the space. In the opposing arch, the tooth that once met the missing tooth in contact begins to erupt — migrating toward the space. Over several years, these movements create bite problems that require orthodontic correction before implant treatment can proceed.

None of this happens overnight. It happens over months and years — quietly, without pain, until the accumulated change is visible on an X-ray and requires correction.

**Timing is not a sales pitch**

When an implant dentist tells you that earlier treatment produces better outcomes, that is a clinical statement, not a close. The biology of bone resorption is well-documented in dental literature. The recommendation to act promptly reflects that documentation.

If you've been putting off addressing a missing tooth, the most useful first step is an evaluation — not a commitment to treatment. Find out where you stand. Understand what's happened to the bone since the extraction. Make a decision from that position.

**Title:** Dental Implants vs. Dentures in Louisville — An Honest Comparison
**Target keywords:** dental implants vs dentures Louisville, All-on-4 vs dentures Louisville KY, replace missing teeth Louisville
**B/E Stage:** B3/E3 — New Worldview (education and contrast)

If you're missing most or all of your teeth, you've probably heard both terms — dental implants and dentures — and you may not have a clear picture of what actually separates them, which is better for whom, and what the real cost comparison looks like over time.

Here is an honest side-by-side.

**How they work**

A traditional removable denture rests on your gum tissue. It's held in place by the shape of your gums and jaw ridge, by suction (particularly for upper dentures), and — over time, increasingly — by adhesive.

Full-arch dental implants (All-on-4 or All-on-X) use four to six titanium posts anchored into the jaw bone. A fixed prosthetic arch of teeth attaches to these posts. The result doesn't come out. It doesn't slide or click. It doesn't require adhesive. It functions like natural teeth and exerts biting force through the bone, which stimulates bone retention rather than allowing continued resorption.

**What you can eat**

With a well-fitting traditional denture: soft foods, most cooked foods, and foods that can be managed with reduced biting force. Hard, tough, or crunchy foods (raw apples, steak, corn on the cob) are typically avoided or eaten carefully to prevent denture movement.

With full-arch implants: no dietary restrictions once healing is complete. Biting force returns to near-normal levels.

**Stability over time**

This is where the comparison gets important.

A traditional denture's fit deteriorates over time — because the jaw bone continues to resorb after tooth loss. As the bone shape changes, the denture fit becomes looser. Most patients need denture relines or replacements every 5–7 years as the jaw shape continues to change.

Full-arch implants slow bone resorption at the implant sites — the presence of the titanium post stimulates bone maintenance in a way that a resting denture cannot. The jaw shape remains more stable. The prosthetic arch typically requires adjustment or replacement after 10–20 years, but the implants themselves typically last a lifetime.

**Cost — short term and long term**

A traditional complete denture costs $1,500–$4,000 per arch in Louisville. It's the lower upfront cost option.

Full-arch implant treatment at a private practice in Louisville costs $25,000–$45,000 per arch.

Over a 20-year horizon, the math changes. Factor in denture relines, replacements, adhesive, and the increasing clinical complexity of treating severe bone loss — and the total cost of the denture pathway is higher than the upfront number suggests.

This doesn't mean implants are the right choice for every patient. For patients who are not good implant candidates due to bone loss, systemic health factors, or age, a well-made denture is a reasonable long-term solution. For patients who are strong implant candidates, the comparison over time typically favors implants.

**Implant-retained dentures: a middle option**

There's a third option that many patients aren't aware of: implant-retained dentures. Two to four implants are placed in the jaw; the existing or new denture snaps onto these implants. The denture is still removable for cleaning, but during the day it's anchored and stable. This option costs $6,000–$18,000 per arch — significantly less than full-arch fixed implants — and delivers dramatically better stability and function than a conventional denture.

For patients who aren't ready for or don't qualify for full-arch fixed implants, implant-retained dentures are often the best next step.
